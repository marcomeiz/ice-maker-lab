Best portable ice maker for your office

I started bringing my own ice maker to the office about a year ago, and people thought I was being ridiculous. Then everyone started using it. The break room was two floors down, the freezer ice trays were always empty or tasted like frozen pizza, and the nearest convenience store was a ten-minute walk. Now, a small countertop ice maker on a side table in my office solved all of that. If you’re thinking about getting one for your workspace, there are a few things that matter a lot more in an office than they do at home. Noise is the big one. Size matters because desk and counter space is limited. Nobody wants to volunteer for cleaning duty, so a self-cleaning function is almost mandatory. And honestly, it should look decent sitting next to your monitor or coffee setup. Here’s what I’d recommend after testing several units in an actual office environment. ## Why noise level matters most

At home, you barely notice the hum of an ice maker over the TV or kitchen activity. In a quiet office, especially if you’re on calls or share space with coworkers, a loud compressor is a dealbreaker. OK so most portable ice makers produce between 35 and 45 decibels during their ice-making cycle. For reference, 35 dB is about the level of a whisper, and 45 dB is closer to a quiet conversation. Anything over 45 dB will be noticeable in a typical office. I’ve written a whole breakdown on [quiet portable ice makers]quietest portable ice makers if noise is your primary concern. The units I’m recommending here all stay under 40 dB during operation. You’ll hear a faint hum when the compressor kicks on, but it shouldn’t interrupt a phone call or a focused work session. ## Size and appearance

Your office ice maker will be visible to coworkers, clients, maybe your boss. A beat-up looking appliance doesn’t send the right message. Look for something with a clean finish, either stainless steel or a matte black or white plastic that doesn’t scream “dorm room.”

Dimensions matter too. Most office desks and side tables can handle something in the range of 9 x 12 x 12 inches. Anything bigger and you’re sacrificing real workspace. Thing is, these machines also need a few inches of clearance on the sides and back for ventilation, so factor that into your layout. ## The self-cleaning question

In a home kitchen, cleaning your ice maker every couple of weeks is no big deal. In an office, it becomes a political issue. Nobody wants to be the person scrubbing out the communal ice maker. That’s why I strongly recommend a model with a one-button self-cleaning cycle. You add water, press the clean button, and it flushes itself out in about 15 minutes. Someone can do it once a week while checking email. No arguments about whose turn it’s. Pick a spot with easy access to a power outlet. These machines don’t need a water line, which is what makes them perfect for an office. You just pour water into the reservoir manually. A standard outlet is all you need. Keep it away from windows with direct sunlight. Heat makes the compressor work harder, which means more noise and slower ice production. A shaded corner or a spot under a cabinet is ideal. But use filtered water if possible. Office tap water varies wildly in quality, and mineral buildup is the number one reason these machines break down prematurely. A simple Brita pitcher next to the ice maker works fine. Empty the melt water at the end of each day, or at least every other day. Most models recirculate melted ice back through the system, which is efficient but means the water gets stale if left over a long weekend. A quick dump and refill on Monday morning keeps everything fresh. ## Is it worth the desk space? I thought it wouldn’t be.
