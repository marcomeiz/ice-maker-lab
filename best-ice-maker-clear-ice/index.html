Best ice maker for clear ice at home

The first time I saw truly clear ice in a cocktail, I thought it was fake. Look, it looked like a glass cube, perfectly transparent, sitting in a tumbler of bourbon like it belonged in a magazine. Then I picked it up and it was real. Cold, heavy, and completely see-through. That’s when I started my obsession with making clear ice at home. Most portable ice makers don’t make clear ice. They make cloudy ice, which is fine for a water bottle or a cooler. But if you care about how your drinks look, how fast the ice melts, or just the satisfaction of holding a perfect crystal cube, you need the right equipment. I’ve spent more time than I’d like to admit testing machines and methods, so let me save you some of that trial and error. ## Why clear ice actually matters

Let’s get the obvious one out of the way: it looks incredible. Clear ice in a glass of whiskey or an old fashioned turns a regular drink into something that feels intentional. Real talk: it’s the difference between a home bar and a home bar that people remember. If you’re making cocktails for friends, clear ice is one of the easiest upgrades you can make. I talk more about that in my best ice makers for cocktails guide. But looks aren’t the only reason. Clear ice melts slower than cloudy ice. That’s not a myth. Cloudy ice is full of tiny air bubbles and trapped impurities. Real talk: those air pockets create more surface area inside the cube, which means heat can penetrate faster and the ice breaks down quicker. A dense, clear cube with no air pockets holds its shape longer. Your drink stays cold without getting watered down as fast. There’s also a purity angle. The cloudiness in regular ice comes from dissolved minerals, gases, and impurities in the water that get trapped as it freezes. So clear ice has had those pushed out during the freezing process. You’re not going to taste the difference in most cases, but if you’re particular about what goes into your drinks, it’s worth knowing. ## The science behind clear ice

Regular ice freezes from all sides at once. Water in an ice tray or a standard ice maker gets cold on every exposed surface simultaneously. As the ice forms inward, it traps air and minerals in the center. That’s why most ice cubes are cloudy in the middle but clearer near the edges. Now, clear ice is made through directional freezing. Instead of freezing from all sides, the water freezes from one direction only, usually from the top down. This pushes the air and impurities downward, away from the forming ice. The result is a block of ice that’s almost entirely free of bubbles and haze. This is the same principle that makes lake ice clear near the surface in winter. Thing is, the top freezes first, pushing everything down into the unfrozen water below. The best clear ice machines and molds replicate this directional freezing on your countertop or in your freezer. The approach varies. Some machines use a slow freezing cycle with insulated sides. Some molds use insulated containers that force freezing from the top. The physics are the same either way. It covers water quality and all the variables that affect clarity. ## Dedicated clear ice makers

These are countertop machines built specifically to produce clear or near-clear ice. They cost more than standard portable ice makers, and they’re slower. But if clear ice is what you’re after, they deliver. ### 1. luma comfort IM200SS clear ice maker

This is the machine I recommend first for anyone serious about clear ice at home. The Luma Comfort uses a slow freezing process with circulating water that mimics directional freezing. The result is ice that’s about 90 to 95 percent clear. Not quite as flawless as what a high-end bar produces with block ice and a chainsaw, but far better than anything a standard portable ice maker can do. It produces about 28 pounds of ice per day, which sounds like a lot until you realize each batch takes significantly longer than a typical ice maker. Fun fact: where a standard machine cranks out a batch in six to ten minutes, the Luma takes closer to 20 to 30 minutes per cycle. The ice comes out as large, clear cubes that are great for cocktails, whiskey, or any drink where you want that visual impact. The downside is size. This machine is bigger than most countertop ice makers, closer to the footprint of a microwave. And it’s not cheap, usually in the $300 to $400 range. But if you’re hosting regularly or you just take your drinks seriously, it’s worth the space and the cost. ### 2. Real talk: newair clearice40 countertop ice maker

NewAir’s ClearIce40 is a more affordable entry point into the clear ice world. It uses a similar principle, circulating water during the freeze cycle to prevent air pockets from forming. The ice isn’t quite as crystal-clear as the Luma Comfort’s output. I’d put it at about 80 to 85 percent clarity. You’ll see a slight haze in the center of some cubes, but the edges and surfaces are transparent. And it makes about 40 pounds of ice per day, which is impressive. The cubes are medium-sized, good for cocktails and highballs but not the big statement cubes you’d use for a rocks pour. The machine runs around $200 to $250, making it a reasonable middle ground between a regular ice maker and the Luma. One thing I noticed: water quality has a bigger impact on this machine’s output than on the Luma. Using filtered or distilled water with the NewAir produces noticeably clearer results. Tap water still gives you better-than-average ice, but if you’re buying this machine specifically for clarity, filtered water is the way to go. ### 3. frigidaire 44 lb chewable ice maker

Wait, hear me out. This one doesn’t make clear cubes, and that’s kind of the point. OK so not everyone wants clear cubes. Some people want clear nugget-style ice, or at least ice that’s clearer than the typical bullet shape from a countertop machine. The Frigidaire’s chewable ice is denser and more compact than standard nugget ice, which means fewer air pockets and a more translucent appearance. It’s not truly clear, but it’s the clearest soft ice you’ll find from a home machine. At about $200, it sits in the same price range as the NewAir. If your goal is clear ice for cocktails with big cubes, skip this one. But if you want the clearest possible version of chewable, everyday ice, it’s a solid option. It shows up in a lot of conversations about bullet vs nugget ice guide too, because it sits in an interesting space between the two. ## Clear ice molds: the budget alternative

You don’t need a dedicated machine to make clear ice. Clear ice molds work on the directional freezing principle and cost between $20 and $60. But they won’t give you the convenience of a countertop machine, but they’ll give you the same quality of ice, sometimes better. ### How they work

Most clear ice molds are insulated containers with an uninsulated opening on top. You fill them with water and put them in your freezer. The insulation prevents the sides and bottom from freezing quickly, so the water freezes from the top down. Air and impurities get pushed to the bottom, where you’ll find a cloudy pocket of ice or unfrozen water. Honestly, you discard that part and keep the clear section. The best molds produce two to six large cubes per cycle. A cycle takes 18 to 24 hours in a standard freezer, so you’re not getting ice on demand. But the cubes themselves are stunning. Completely transparent and slow-melting. ### Best clear ice molds

The True Cubes clear ice mold is my favorite. It makes four 2-inch cubes per batch, and they come out remarkably clear. The silicone mold separates the clear section from the cloudy section cleanly. It costs about $40. For spheres, the Wintersmiths Ice Baller is the one to get. It produces a single large sphere of clear ice, about 2.5 inches in diameter. It’s more of a specialty item, but if you serve a lot of spirit-forward cocktails, a clear sphere’s a real showpiece. It runs about $50 to $60. The OnTheRocks Ice Tray is a budget pick at around $20. It makes two large cubes per batch. Real talk: they’re not quite as clear as what the True Cubes produces, but for the price, it’s a great entry point. ## Tips for clearer ice from any machine

Even if you’re not buying a dedicated clear ice maker or a directional freezing mold, you can improve the clarity of ice from your current machine. Here are some things that actually work. Use distilled or filtered water. This is the single biggest improvement you can make. Most of the cloudiness in ice comes from dissolved minerals and chlorine in tap water. Remove those before freezing, and you’ll get noticeably clearer results. Worth mentioning: it won’t be crystal-clear, but it’ll be better. Boil the water first. Boiling drives out dissolved gases, which are responsible for a lot of the tiny bubbles trapped in cloudy ice. Some people boil twice: boil, let it cool, then boil again. I’ve tested this, and the double boil does produce slightly clearer ice, though the improvement is marginal. OK so slow down the freezing. If your freezer has an adjustable thermostat, raise the temperature slightly. Slower freezing allows air bubbles to escape rather than getting trapped. This doesn’t work well for portable ice makers since they control their own temperature, but it’s effective for tray ice. Keep your machine clean. Mineral buildup inside the machine can reintroduce impurities into the water during the freezing cycle. Regular cleaning, whether through the self-clean function or manual scrubbing, helps maintain ice clarity. I keep my own machines on a regular cleaning schedule, and I can see the difference in the ice when I slack off. ## My recommendation

For the best clear ice at home, get the Luma Comfort IM200SS if your budget allows. It produces large, genuinely clear cubes without any extra effort. Honestly, fill the reservoir, press start, and let it do its thing. If you’re on a tighter budget or you only need clear ice occasionally, buy a True Cubes mold. The $40 investment pays for itself the first time you serve drinks with perfect ice and someone asks how you made it. And if you already have a standard portable ice maker that you love from my 9 best portable ice makers list, don’t throw it out. Use distilled water and keep the machine clean. You’ll get ice that’s 30 to 40 percent clearer than what you’re making now. It won’t be bar-quality clear, but it’ll be a meaningful upgrade. Clear ice might seem like a small thing. But drinks are about the details. The right glass, the right pour, the right ice. Now, get those right, and a Tuesday night bourbon feels like an event. That’s reason enough for me.
