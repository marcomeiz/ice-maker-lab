Euhomy ice maker review after 6 months of daily use

I bought the Euhomy countertop ice maker back in November because I was tired of filling ice trays and I didn’t want to spend more than $100. Now, six months later, I’ve run this thing almost every single day. Some days twice. I’ve made ice for house parties, random Tuesday afternoon iced coffees, and coolers full of drinks for weekend cookouts. Here’s what I actually think of it after half a year of real use. ## What you get in the box

The packaging is nothing fancy. A brown box with some foam inserts, the ice maker itself, an ice scoop, an ice basket that sits inside the machine, a small drain plug, and a manual that’s surprisingly readable. Setup took me about three minutes. You fill the water tank, plug it in, pick your ice size, and press start. Real talk: that’s it. There’s no water line to hook up, no installation, no tools. I had it sitting on my kitchen counter making ice within five minutes of opening the box. If you’ve never used a countertop ice maker before, you’ll appreciate how dead simple this is. I placed it near an outlet with a few inches of clearance on the sides for ventilation, and it’s been in that same spot ever since. ## How much ice does it actually make? But euhomy claims 26 pounds of ice per day. I don’t buy that number. In my experience, running it consistently throughout the day, I’d estimate it produces closer to 20 pounds. Maybe 22 on a good day when the kitchen isn’t too warm. The first batch of ice comes out in about 6 to 8 minutes, which is genuinely fast. Fun fact: you get nine bullet-shaped pieces per cycle. If you’re picking the small size, the cycles run a bit quicker. Large ice takes a couple more minutes per batch. Fun fact: for a single person or a couple, 20 pounds a day is more than enough. I’ve never actually run out of ice during normal daily use. The only time I came close was during a Fourth of July party when I was filling a large cooler. I started the machine early that morning and it kept up fine, but I wouldn’t rely on it as the sole ice source for a big gathering. If you’re comparing options across this price range, I put together a list of the 9 best portable ice makers that covers several models side by side. ## Ice quality and shape

The Euhomy makes bullet-style ice, not nugget ice, not crescent, not clear cubes. And bullet ice. It’s hollow in the center, cylinder-shaped, and it melts faster than solid cubes from a freezer. That’s just the nature of countertop ice makers at this price. Look, you get two size options: small and large. The small pieces are good for blended drinks and water bottles. The large ones work better for glasses of whiskey or cocktails where you don’t want the ice disappearing in two minutes. I use the large setting about 80% of the time. The ice won’t win any beauty contests. Real talk: it’s functional. Fun fact: it chills drinks. It does the job. But if you’re expecting crystal-clear, slow-melting cubes like you’d get from a high-end machine, you’ll be disappointed. That said, for the price, I think the ice quality is perfectly acceptable. ## How fast does the ice melt in the basket? This is worth talking about because nobody mentions it in their quick reviews. The ice basket sits inside the machine but it’s not a freezer. There’s no insulation. So the ice starts melting pretty quickly once it’s been sitting there. On a warm day, I’d say you’ve got about two to three hours before the basket is more water than ice. But on cooler days or if your kitchen stays around 68 degrees, you might stretch that to four hours. Thing is, the melted water drains back into the reservoir and gets recycled into new ice, which is a smart design choice. But if you make a bunch of ice and leave it sitting, you’ll come back to a half-empty basket. My workaround is simple. I transfer finished ice into a freezer bag and toss it in the freezer. Then the machine keeps running and I keep stacking bags. By the time guests show up, I’ve got plenty stored away. ## Noise level: noticeable but not awful

I’m not going to pretend this thing is silent. It’s not. Here’s the thing: when the compressor kicks on, you hear it. There’s a steady hum plus occasional gurgling sounds when water cycles through. Fun fact: if you’re sitting in the same room watching TV, you’ll notice it. If it’s running in the kitchen while you’re in the living room, it fades into background noise. I measured it at roughly 40 to 45 decibels during operation, which puts it somewhere between a quiet library and normal conversation. It’s louder than a good refrigerator but quieter than a dishwasher. At night, I turn it off. I wouldn’t want it running in a studio apartment while I’m trying to sleep. If noise is a dealbreaker for you, I’d honestly say skip countertop ice makers entirely. Now, they all make some noise. This one isn’t the worst offender, but it’s not whisper-quiet either. ## The self-cleaning feature

The Euhomy has a self-cleaning mode, and it works OK. You press and hold a button, and it runs water through the system for about 10 to 15 minutes. I run it once a week with plain water and once a month with a vinegar and water mix. Honestly, does it replace a proper deep clean? No. Quick note: i still take the basket out and wipe down the interior by hand every couple of weeks. The self-clean function is more of a rinse cycle than a true cleaning. It helps prevent buildup between manual cleanings, but if you rely on it exclusively, you’ll start getting funky-tasting ice after a month or two. For a full breakdown on keeping these machines in good shape, check out my guide on how to clean your ice maker. Fun fact: it covers the vinegar method and some other tricks I’ve picked up. ## Build quality: cheap but functional

I’ll be honest. This thing feels like a $90 appliance. The housing is all plastic. And it’s lightweight. It doesn’t feel premium in any way. When you tap on the side, it sounds hollow. But here’s the thing: it works. Honestly, it’s been working for six months straight without any mechanical issues. No leaks. No error codes. The compressor hasn’t given me trouble. The water pump still runs fine. Look, functionally, it’s been reliable despite not feeling like it should be. The one complaint I’ve about build quality is the ice scoop. Thing is, the handle feels flimsy and I keep expecting it to snap when I’m digging into a full basket. It hasn’t broken yet, but I wouldn’t be shocked if it does eventually. I bought a cheap metal scoop as a backup and I’d recommend you do the same. Quick note: the control panel on top is straightforward. Two buttons, a couple of indicator lights, and that’s it. Nothing has worn out or stopped working on the panel after six months of daily use. ## Water tank and capacity

The reservoir holds about 2.2 liters of water, which produces roughly a basket and a half of ice before you need to refill. I find myself adding water two or three times a day during heavy use. Thing is, on a normal day where I’m just making ice for personal use, one fill in the morning usually carries me through. So there’s a window on the side so you can check the water level without opening anything. When the water gets low, an indicator light turns on and the machine stops automatically. It won’t run dry and damage itself, which is a nice touch for a budget machine. Filling it’s easy. And you just open the top lid and pour water directly into the reservoir. No special container needed. I use a pitcher from my Brita filter, which takes about 10 seconds. ## Six-Month durability report

After 180-ish days of near-daily use, here’s where things stand. The machine runs like it did on day one. Worth mentioning: no decline in ice production speed. No weird noises that weren’t there before. No parts have broken or needed replacement. Fun fact: the exterior has a few minor scuffs from being on my counter near other kitchen items, but nothing that affects performance. The rubber feet on the bottom still grip well. The lid hinge feels the same as when it was new. The only wear I’ve noticed is on that ice scoop I mentioned. The plastic where the handle meets the scoop part has developed a slight flex that wasn’t there originally. It still works, but I give it maybe another six months before it cracks. Like I said, grab a metal one. I also noticed the self-clean cycle seems slightly louder than it was initially, but that could be my imagination. OK so the ice-making cycles themselves sound the same. ## Euhomy vs. Silonn: how do they compare? OK so these two machines get compared constantly, and for good reason. They look almost identical, have nearly the same specs, and compete at the same price point. I haven’t personally used the Silonn long-term, but I’ve tested one at a friend’s house and read enough about both to have an opinion. The Euhomy is usually a few dollars cheaper, which is why I went with it. For ice production, noise, and ice quality, they’re practically interchangeable. Here’s the thing: the Silonn might have a slightly sleeker design, but we’re talking about minor cosmetic differences. If you’re stuck between the two, it mostly comes down to whichever one is cheaper on the day you’re shopping. I don’t think you’d regret either choice. For more budget options, my best ice makers under /ice-maker-lab/best-ice-maker-under-100/00 roundup covers several machines in this range including both the Euhomy and Silonn. ## Who should buy the euhomy ice maker

This machine is built for budget buyers who want reliable daily ice without spending a lot. Quick note: if you’re a college student, someone in a small apartment, an RV traveler, or just somebody who hates ice trays, the Euhomy gets the job done at a price that won’t hurt. It’s also good for anyone who needs a secondary ice source. If your fridge makes ice but not enough for entertaining, this fills the gap nicely. Thing is, i use my fridge’s ice maker for everyday stuff and fire up the Euhomy when I need extra volume. People who value simplicity will appreciate this machine too. There’s basically nothing to figure out. Fill, press, wait, scoop. My mom used it at my place without any instructions and had zero issues. ## Who should skip it

If you want nugget ice, look elsewhere. And the Euhomy makes bullet ice only, and no software update or hack is going to change that. Nugget ice machines cost significantly more, but if that’s what you’re after, bullet ice won’t satisfy you. If you need quiet operation, this isn’t your machine. It’s manageable noise, but it’s definitely there. Worth mentioning: open-plan living spaces or studio apartments where the kitchen is right next to your sleeping area could be a problem. And if you’re buying for a large family or frequent large parties as your primary use case, you might want something with a bigger reservoir and higher actual output. The Euhomy is built for light to moderate use, not commercial-scale production. ## Final verdict

Six months in, I’m satisfied with the Euhomy ice maker. It’s not fancy. It doesn’t pretend to be. But it makes ice quickly, it hasn’t broken down, and it cost me less than dinner for two at a decent restaurant. The 26-pound daily claim is exaggerated, the scoop feels like it’ll snap someday, and the ice melts fast in the basket. Those are real drawbacks. But for under $100, I think the Euhomy delivers solid value. Here’s the thing: it does exactly what it promises at a price that makes it hard to complain. If your expectations are in check and you just want a simple, affordable ice maker that works every day, this is a safe pick.
