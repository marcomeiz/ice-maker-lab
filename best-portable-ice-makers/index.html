9 best portable ice makers in 2025

I bought nine portable ice makers, ran them all on my kitchen counter for two weeks, and measured everything I could think of. If you’re looking for the best portable ice maker, the GE Profile Opal is the one I’d buy again. But it’s not the right pick for everyone, so here’s the full breakdown. ## 1. GE profile opal: the best portable ice maker overall

Price: Around $480

The Opal is the machine I kept on my counter after testing was done. That tells you everything. It makes nugget ice. Real, chewable, Sonic-style nugget ice. So not the bullet-shaped pieces you get from cheaper machines. The texture is soft, crunchy, and perfect for drinks. My wife, who doesn’t care about any of this stuff, noticed the difference immediately. Look, it connects to WiFi so you can start a batch from your phone. I thought that was gimmicky until I started hitting the button from bed at 6 AM so ice would be ready by the time I got to the kitchen. The side tank holds about 3 pounds of ice, and it produces roughly 24 pounds per day. Now, what sucks: it’s loud. Not “can’t watch TV” loud, but you’ll hear it running. And the price. $480 is a lot for a countertop ice maker. Real talk: you’re paying a premium for nugget ice and the GE name. Who it’s for: anyone who wants the best ice quality and doesn’t mind spending more to get it. If you want nugget ice specifically, check out my full list of best nugget ice makers picks too. ## 2. Silonn SLIM01: best budget portable ice maker

Price: Around $90

The Silonn was the surprise of this test. At under $100, I expected it to feel cheap and break within a week. Real talk: it didn’t. It pumps out its first batch of bullet-shaped ice in about 6 minutes. That’s fast. The two size options (small and large) are nice, though the “large” size is still pretty small compared to freezer ice cubes. Fun fact: the 26-pound daily capacity held up in my testing, getting close to that number when I ran it continuously. Now, the reservoir holds about 2 liters, so you’ll refill it a few times a day if you’re using it heavily. The scoop that comes with it’s flimsy plastic, but it works. What sucks: no self-cleaning cycle. You’ll need to clean it manually, and I recommend doing that weekly. Here’s my guide on how to clean your ice maker if you need help with that. Honestly, the ice also melts fast since there’s no insulation in the basket. Who it’s for: someone who wants cheap, fast ice without overthinking it. It’s one of the best picks if you’re shopping on a budget, and I compared it against other options in my best ice makers under /ice-maker-lab/best-ice-maker-under-100/00 guide. ## 3. Euhomy IM-02: best for everyday use

Price: Around $110

The Euhomy sits right in the sweet spot between cheap and overpriced. Build quality feels solid. Look, the stainless steel exterior doesn’t show fingerprints the way I expected, and the control panel is straightforward. It makes 26 pounds of ice per day with two size settings. Here’s the thing: first batch comes in about 8 minutes. What I liked most is the self-cleaning function. You press one button and add some water out. That alone puts it above several machines on this list. The ice basket is removable, which makes scooping easier than reaching into a fixed bin. And the indicator lights for “add water” and “ice full” are bright enough to see from across the room. What sucks: the fan on the back needs clearance. But i had it pushed against the wall initially and it ran warm. So give it 6 inches of space behind it. The drain plug on the bottom is also a pain to access. Who it’s for: families who want a reliable daily ice maker that doesn’t cost GE Opal money. ## 4. Real talk: gE profile opal 2.0 with side tank: the upgrade pick

Price: Around $580

Yes, two GE Opals on this list. The 2.0 version adds a side tank that boosts ice storage capacity significantly. You get about 3x the ice holding capacity compared to the original. Everything I said about the first Opal applies here. Same great nugget ice. Look, same WiFi connectivity. Same noise. The side tank clips onto the machine and acts as extra storage, so ice builds up there instead of sitting in the small internal bin. What sucks: the price jump over the standard Opal isn’t huge, but you’re now pushing $600 for a countertop appliance. The side tank also takes up more counter space, obviously. And if the tank isn’t seated correctly, water drips down the side. I learned that the hard way. Thing is, who it’s for: people who already know they want nugget ice and go through a lot of it. But entertaining, parties, offices. ## 5. Frigidaire EFIC108: best compact portable ice maker

Price: Around $115

The Frigidaire is small. That’s its main selling point. If your counter space is limited or you want something you can toss in an RV or take to a tailgate, this one makes sense. But it weighs about 20 pounds and has a handle on top. The 26-pound daily output matches bigger machines, though in practice I got closer to 20 pounds. Ice comes in two sizes and the first cycle takes about 7 minutes. The build feels decent for the price. Not premium, but not flimsy either. It ran without issues for the full two weeks of testing. What sucks: the ice bin is small. You’ll be transferring ice to a cooler or freezer bag often if you’re making a lot. OK so there’s no self-cleaning mode. And the compressor can run hot, so keep it out of direct sunlight. Who it’s for: RV owners, tailgaters, or anyone working with limited counter space who still wants a capable machine. ## 6. Real talk: igloo ICEB26: best for portability

Price: Around $130

Igloo has been making coolers forever, so it makes sense they’d make a decent ice machine. The ICEB26 is built to move around. It’s 23 pounds with a sturdy carry handle. Worth mentioning: production rate is solid at 26 pounds per day. It makes bullet-style ice in two sizes, and the larger size is actually decent. Not as big as a freezer cube, but close enough that drinks don’t water down instantly. The window on top lets you check ice levels without opening the lid, which is a small detail I appreciated. Less heat gets in, less ice melts. What sucks: it’s noisier than the Silonn and Euhomy at comparable price points. OK so the water reservoir is only 2.2 liters, so you’re refilling often. And the included ice scoop feels like an afterthought. Who it’s for: someone who needs to move their ice maker between locations regularly. Look, beach house, boat, camping. ## 7. And newair AI-100: best portable ice maker for the price

Price: Around $130

The Newair AI-100 has been around for years and there’s a reason it’s still selling. It’s reliable. Not exciting, not flashy, just consistent. It makes 28 pounds of ice per day, which is a bit above average for this group. Three ice size options instead of the usual two. The medium setting produced my favorite bullet ice in this price range. Fun fact: not too small, not too hollow. Setup took about two minutes. Plug it in, fill the reservoir, press the button. First ice in 7 minutes. But what sucks: the design looks dated. Look, if aesthetics matter to you, this will stick out next to modern kitchen appliances. Not the prettiest, but dependable. ## 8. Now, fREE VILLAGE portable ice maker

I didn’t just read spec sheets. I bought every machine on this list (some with my own money, some provided by brands) and ran them all in my garage and kitchen for two weeks in July. Texas heat. Real conditions. Here’s what I tracked:

  • Time to first ice batch
  • Total ice produced in 24 hours of continuous running
  • Noise level at 3 feet (measured with a decibel meter)
  • Ice quality, meaning size, density, how fast it melted
  • How often I had to refill the water
  • Ease of cleaning
  • Whether it still worked after two weeks of daily use

I also had four people in my household using these machines daily, so the feedback isn’t just mine. Quick note: my kids preferred the nugget ice from the Opal. My wife liked the Euhomy because it was quiet. I personally cared most about ice quality and reliability. And machines that broke, leaked, or produced slushy ice got knocked down the list. Machines that just worked, day after day, got moved up. ## What to look for in the best portable ice maker

Ice type matters more than you think. Bullet ice is fine for water and soft drinks. Nugget ice is better for cocktails and just more fun to chew. Know which one you want before you buy. Now, daily capacity numbers are theoretical. Every manufacturer claims 26 pounds per day, but real-world output depends on room temperature, how often you open the lid, and how full the reservoir is. Expect about 75-80% of the advertised number. Self-cleaning is worth paying for. Fun fact: portable ice makers grow mold and mineral buildup fast, especially if you use tap water. A self-cleaning cycle doesn’t replace manual deep cleaning, but it cuts the frequency from weekly to every two weeks or so. Noise is hard to judge from online specs. Most of these machines run between 35-45 decibels, which sounds quiet on paper. In a small kitchen, though, 45 decibels is noticeable. Real talk: if noise bothers you, prioritize the Euhomy or Silonn. Size and weight matter if you plan to move it. Some of these machines are 30+ pounds, which makes them “portable” in name only. If you’re taking it to a cabin, RV, or tailgate, check the weight and dimensions first. Counter space is real. These machines need room behind them for ventilation. Thing is, budget at least 6 inches of clearance on the back and sides. Real talk: measure your counter before ordering. ## My final recommendation

If money isn’t the issue, get the GE Profile Opal. The nugget ice is genuinely better than anything else on this list, and the WiFi scheduling is surprisingly useful once you get used to it. If you want something solid under $100, the Silonn SLIM01 is the one I’d pick. Honestly, it’s fast and hasn’t given anyone problems in the months since I tested it. And if you want the best balance of features and price, the Euhomy IM-02 is the smart middle-ground pick. But self-cleaning, decent build, and ice quality that punches above its price point. Buy the one that fits how you’ll actually use it, not the one with the most Amazon reviews.
