How much electricity does an ice maker use?

2025-05-20 · Guides and tips

Fun fact:

How much electricity does an ice maker use? if you’re thinking about buying a portable ice maker, the electricity cost is probably on your mind. good news: these machines are surprisingly cheap to run. most portable ice makers draw between 100 and 200 watts, which translates to roughly $1 to $3 per month on your electric bill. let’s break down the actual numbers so you know exactly what to expect. ## typical wattage ranges by ice maker type

Not all ice makers pull the same amount of power. Now, the wattage depends on the type of machine, its size, and what kind of ice it produces. Here’s a general breakdown:

  • Bullet ice makers (standard countertop): 100 to 150 watts. These are the most common portable models. They produce small, hollow ice bullets and are the most energy-efficient option for home use. - Nugget ice makers: 150 to 250 watts. Nugget (or “sonic”) ice requires a different freezing and scraping mechanism, which draws more power. The tradeoff is that soft, chewable ice that people love. - Commercial/large-capacity models: 300 watts and up. Now, these are built for offices, bars, or anyone who needs a high daily output. So they run longer cycles and use beefier compressors. You’ll find the exact [wattage listed on the spec sheet]ice maker wattage guide of any model you’re considering. Always check that number before buying, especially if you plan to run the machine off a battery or power station. ## Monthly cost calculation: doing the math

Let’s say you’ve a standard bullet ice maker that draws 120 watts. Here’s how to figure out your monthly electricity cost. Step 1: Convert watts to kilowatts. 120 watts / 1,000 = 0.12 kW

Step 2: Estimate daily run time. Most portable ice makers don’t run 24 hours a day. And a typical household might run one for 8 hours to fill a cooler or keep the ice bucket stocked. Let’s use 8 hours. Step 3: Calculate daily energy use. 0.12 kW x 8 hours = 0.96 kWh per day

Step 4: Calculate monthly energy use. 0.96 kWh x 30 days = 28.8 kWh per month

Step 5: Multiply by your electricity rate. At the U.S. Now, average of $0.12 per kWh: 28.8 kWh x $0.12 = $3.46 per month

That’s the high end for a standard model running 8 hours every single day. If you only run it a few times a week, you’re looking at closer to $1 per month. Honestly, for a nugget ice maker pulling 200 watts at the same 8-hour schedule: 0.20 kW x 8 hours x 30 days x $0.12 = $5.76 per month

Still not bad. Even the most power-hungry portable model won’t make a meaningful dent in your electric bill. ## How does it compare to other appliances? Look, here’s some perspective. A portable ice maker uses less electricity than most appliances already in your kitchen:

Let’s put this in context. The average U.S. Household electric bill is around $130 per month. Adding a portable ice maker that costs $3 to $4 per month is a 2-3% increase. You’d barely notice it. Even if you ran a nugget ice maker for 12 hours a day, every day, you’re still under $9 per month. That’s less than a single bag of ice from the gas station, and you’d be making far more ice than one bag. Thing is, the machines that do rack up electricity costs are built-in undercounter models and commercial units that run around the clock. But those aren’t what most people are shopping for. Standard countertop portables are about as cheap to run as it gets. ## Tips to reduce power use

If you want to keep electricity costs at the absolute minimum, here are a few practical things you can do:

Don’t run it 24/7. Portable ice makers don’t have long-term storage. Quick note: the ice melts back into the reservoir and gets recycled. Fun fact: running the machine nonstop wastes energy producing ice that just melts again. Make ice in batches, transfer it to a freezer or cooler, and turn the machine off. Pre-chill your water. The colder the water going in, the less work the compressor has to do. Using cold tap water or refrigerated water instead of room-temperature water can cut cycle times by 20-30%. Shorter cycles mean less electricity. Real talk: Keep it out of direct sunlight. Heat is the enemy of efficiency. A machine sitting in the sun or next to a hot stove works harder to freeze water. Put it somewhere cool with good airflow around the vents. Clean it regularly. Scale buildup on the evaporator makes the machine work harder. A quick cleaning with white vinegar every couple of weeks keeps everything running at peak efficiency. Look, Choose the right size for your needs. If you only need ice for a couple of drinks, a 26-pound-per-day model is plenty. Buying an oversized machine means a bigger compressor drawing more watts than you actually need. ## Running an ice maker off-grid: RV and camping use

This is where wattage really matters. When you’re running off a battery, solar panels, or a portable power station, every watt counts. Let’s say you’re [camping with a portable ice maker]best ice makers for camping and you’ve a 500Wh power station. Here’s the thing: honestly, here’s how long you can run different models:

  • 100W ice maker: 500Wh / 100W = 5 hours of run time
  • 150W ice maker: 500Wh / 150W = 3.3 hours of run time
  • 200W nugget maker: 500Wh / 200W = 2.5 hours of run time

In practice, you’ll get slightly less than these numbers because of inverter efficiency losses (usually around 85-90%). So that 100W machine on a 500Wh battery realistically gives you about 4 to 4.5 hours. That’s enough time to make 15 to 20 pounds of ice with a standard model. More than enough for a weekend trip if you store the ice in a good cooler. For [RV use]best ice makers for RVs, you’ve got more options. Most RVs have a 30-amp or 50-amp shore power connection, which handles a portable ice maker without breaking a sweat. If you’re boondocking on battery power, a 100W solar panel paired with a lithium battery bank can keep a small ice maker running for a few hours each day. Quick note: here’s a quick formula for calculating battery run time with any setup:

Battery capacity (Wh) x 0.85 (inverter efficiency) / ice maker wattage = hours of run time

If you’re buying a power station specifically for running an ice maker, aim for at least 500Wh capacity. That gives you a comfortable buffer and lets you run other small devices at the same time. ## The bottom line

Portable ice makers are one of the cheapest appliances to run. At 100 to 200 watts, you’re looking at $1 to $6 per month depending on the model and how often you use it. That’s a fraction of what your refrigerator costs to operate. OK so the biggest factor in your electricity cost isn’t the machine itself. It’s how many hours per day you run it. Worth mentioning: make ice in batches and turn the machine off when you don’t need it. You’ll keep your costs at the low end of that range without any effort. For off-grid use, pair a low-wattage model with a decent power station, and you’ll have cold drinks wherever you go without draining your battery bank.
