Best quiet portable ice maker for home

I love my portable ice maker. What I don’t love is the sound it makes at 2 a.m. When I’ve left it running on the kitchen counter. If you’ve ever been jolted awake by the hum and clunk of a countertop ice machine, you know exactly what I’m talking about. Here’s the thing: most people don’t think about noise until they’ve already bought a machine. Then they plug it in and realize it sounds like a tiny refrigerator is having a nervous breakdown three feet from where they eat breakfast. That’s why I put together this list of the quietest portable ice makers you can actually buy right now. ## Why noise matters more than you think

Open floor plans have taken over. The kitchen bleeds into the living room, which bleeds into the dining area. There’s no door to close. When your ice maker is running, everyone hears it. You hear it during movie night. So you hear it during phone calls. Your dog hears it and tilts his head like something’s wrong. It’s even worse in apartments. Thin walls, small spaces, and neighbors who already think you’re too loud. I lived in a studio for two years, and my first portable ice maker was so loud I could only run it during the day when I wasn’t home. And that defeated the whole purpose. Offices are another common spot for these machines. A break room with a loud ice maker is annoying. A shared workspace with one? That’s a recipe for passive-aggressive sticky notes. And if your kitchen is anywhere near a bedroom, forget about running a loud machine at night. You’ll either wake someone up or you’ll wake yourself up. Neither is great. ## How loud are portable ice makers, really? Most portable ice makers run somewhere between 40 and 60 decibels. To put that in perspective, 40 dB is about the level of a quiet library. 50 dB is moderate rainfall. But 60 dB is a normal conversation. Sounds harmless, right? The problem is that these machines don’t produce a steady, even hum. They cycle. The compressor kicks on, runs for a while, then shuts off. The water pump activates. Ice drops into the basket with a clatter. But then the whole cycle starts again. It’s the inconsistency that makes it annoying. Your brain can tune out constant white noise, but it can’t ignore sudden changes in sound. I’ve measured quite a few machines with a decibel meter, and the numbers manufacturers list are almost always taken during the quietest part of the cycle. The actual peak noise during ice ejection or compressor startup is usually 5 to 10 dB higher than what’s on the spec sheet. Keep that in mind when you’re comparing models. ## What makes one ice maker quieter than another

The compressor is the biggest factor. Cheap compressors vibrate more and run louder. Higher-quality compressors, especially those with better mounting and dampening, produce less noise. Some brands use the same compressor suppliers as mini fridges, and you can tell the difference. Insulation matters too. Machines with thicker walls and better internal padding absorb more of the compressor’s vibration. This is one of those areas where you tend to get what you pay for. A $90 machine probably has thinner plastic housing than a $180 one. But the ice ejection mechanism plays a role as well. Some machines push ice off the freezing prongs with a gentle sweep, while others basically fling it into the basket. The ones that fling tend to be louder during the drop phase. Fan quality is the last piece. Every portable ice maker has a small fan to help with heat dissipation. Cheap fans wobble. Wobbling fans make noise. Fun fact: good fans don’t. ## The 5 quietest portable ice makers i’ve found

I tested or extensively researched each of these models. The dB readings I’m sharing are based on my own measurements where possible and verified user reports where not. All readings were taken at a distance of about three feet, which is roughly how far you’d be if the machine were sitting on your counter. ### 1. OK so ecozy portable ice maker (about 38 db)

This one genuinely surprised me. At around 38 decibels during normal operation, the Ecozy is the quietest countertop ice maker I’ve come across. The compressor is well-insulated, and the ice ejection is smooth and gentle. You can hear it if you’re standing right next to it, but from across the room, it basically disappears into the background. It makes bullet-style ice in two sizes, produces about 26 pounds per day, and has a self-cleaning function. For a quiet portable ice maker, this is the one I’d recommend first. ### 2. GE profile opal 2.0 nugget ice maker (about 40 db)

The Opal is famous for its nugget ice, and it’s also one of the quieter machines out there. Real talk: gE put some thought into the compressor and housing design. At around 40 dB, it’s about as loud as a quiet room with a ceiling fan on low. The trade-off is price. This thing costs significantly more than a standard countertop ice maker, usually north of $500. But if you want nugget ice without the noise, it’s the best option available. It also connects to a water line if you want continuous production, which means fewer fill-and-refill cycles that generate extra sound. ### 3. silonn countertop ice maker (about 42 db)

I’ve written about the Silonn before in my Silonn ice maker review, and noise is one of the reasons I keep coming back to it. At roughly 42 dB, it’s not the absolute quietest, but it’s well below average. OK so what I like is that the compressor transitions are smooth. There’s no jarring startup sound. It just kind of hums along. For the price point, usually under $120, it’s hard to beat. Makes nine ice cubes per cycle in about six to eight minutes. Fun fact: solid machine for apartments and small kitchens. ### 4. frigidaire EFIC117 (about 43 db)

Frigidaire is a name people trust, and this model earns that trust on the noise front. At about 43 dB, it sits comfortably in the “you’ll notice it but it won’t bother you” range. The build quality is decent, and the compressor doesn’t rattle. It produces up to 26 pounds of ice per day and comes with a basket and scoop. Nothing fancy, but reliable and quiet enough for an office, dorm room, or kitchen that’s close to a living area. If you’re looking for a machine for a shared space, check out my picks for best ice makers for offices too. ### 5. Now, newair countertop ice maker (about 44 db)

The NewAir rounds out this list at roughly 44 dB, which is still below the average for this category. It’s a straightforward machine: plug it in, add water, press start. What keeps it quiet is a well-balanced fan and a compressor that doesn’t spike much during startup. It makes bullet ice in two sizes and can produce about 28 pounds per day, which is slightly above average. It’s not as whisper-quiet as the Ecozy, but it’s a solid pick if that model is out of stock or out of your price range. ## Tips for reducing noise from any portable ice maker

Even if you buy one of the quieter models, placement matters. Put the machine on a solid, flat surface. Uneven counters cause vibration. If your counter is slightly off-level, stick a small rubber pad or silicone mat under the machine. This alone can knock a few decibels off. Keep it away from walls. The compressor vents heat out the back or sides, and if that air can’t flow freely, the fan works harder and gets louder. Real talk: give it at least four to six inches of clearance on all sides. Don’t let the water reservoir run low. When the pump sucks air because there’s not enough water, it makes a gurgling sound that’s louder than normal operation. Top it off before that happens. And empty the ice basket regularly. When ice piles up and new cubes drop onto a full basket, the clatter is louder. Look, a half-full basket absorbs the impact better than an overflowing one. ## Which one should you pick? If noise is your top priority, the Ecozy at 38 dB is the clear winner. It’s affordable, effective, and genuinely quiet. If you want nugget ice specifically, the GE Opal 2.0 is worth the premium. It’s quiet, the ice is fantastic, and it’s built to last. For a budget pick, the Silonn is the best balance of noise, price, and performance. It shows up on my list of 9 best portable ice makers for good reason. If you’re putting a machine in a dorm, noise is probably high on your list too. I’ve got a separate guide on best ice makers for dorms that covers what to look for in tight spaces with thin walls. The bottom line: you don’t have to put up with a loud ice maker. The technology has gotten better, and the quieter models don’t cost dramatically more than the loud ones. Worth mentioning: pay attention to the decibel range, read user reviews that mention noise specifically, and give your machine room to breathe. Your ears will thank you.
