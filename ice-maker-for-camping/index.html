Best portable ice maker for camping trips

I brought a portable ice maker for camping last summer and it changed the whole trip. Cold drinks at the campsite without running to the gas station every morning. No soggy cooler full of melted bag ice by day two. Just fresh ice whenever I wanted it. But I’ll be straight with you: this only works if you plan your power situation ahead of time. ## You need power. And period. And let’s get the big thing out of the way first. A portable ice maker runs on electricity. There’s no hand-crank version. No solar-only option. If you’re backpacking into the wilderness with nothing but a tent and a headlamp, this isn’t for you. Now, you’ve got three realistic options for powering an ice maker at a campsite:

A portable power station like a Jackery or EcoFlow. OK so these are battery packs you charge at home and bring along. Most decent ones can run an ice maker for several hours. A generator. Louder, heavier, but unlimited runtime as long as you’ve fuel. Some campgrounds don’t allow them, so check first. A campsite electrical hookup. Look, plenty of state parks and KOA campgrounds have powered sites. Plug in and you’re good. This is the easiest path by far. If you don’t have any of these, you don’t have an ice maker. That’s the honest truth. But if you already own a power station for your camping setup, or you tend to book powered sites, an ice maker is a surprisingly practical add-on. Honestly, for a deeper look at energy draw, check out our guide on ice maker electricity costs. ## What to look for in a camping ice maker

Not every countertop ice maker belongs in the outdoors. I’ve hauled a few different models to campsites over the past couple years, and the ones that work well out there share a few traits. Low wattage matters more than anything. You want something that draws under 150 watts, ideally closer to 100 watts during operation. That’s the difference between draining your power station in three hours versus getting a full day of ice production. Weight counts when you’re loading a car. OK so anything over 25 pounds starts to feel like a pain, especially if you’re already packing a cooler and the rest of it. Fun fact: the sweet spot is 18 to 23 pounds. Speed is nice but not everything. Most portable ice makers produce a batch in 6 to 10 minutes. That’s fast enough. Don’t pay extra for a machine that shaves off two minutes per cycle. Honestly, durability is underrated. A flimsy plastic housing might be fine on your kitchen counter, but it won’t survive getting bumped around in the back of a truck. Look for something that feels solid when you pick it up. Noise level can matter at a quiet campsite. So most ice makers hum along at around 35 to 45 decibels, which is about the level of a quiet conversation. And that’s fine. A few models are louder. Avoid those if you camp near other people. ## 5 portable ice makers that work well for camping

I’ve tested or researched these models specifically with camping in mind. They’re all countertop bullet-style ice makers, which is the most common and practical type for this use. Worth mentioning: for a broader comparison, see our full 9 best portable ice makers roundup. ### Euhomy IM-F countertop ice maker

This one’s become a favorite in the camping community and I understand why. Worth mentioning: it draws about 100 watts during ice-making cycles, which is low enough for most 500Wh power stations. It weighs around 19 pounds and produces 26 pounds of ice per day in ideal conditions. The first batch comes out in about 8 minutes. It’s not fancy, but it’s reliable, and the price sits well under $100 most of the time. The compact footprint also means it fits on a camp table without taking over. ### Silonn SLIM01 compact ice maker

Silonn’s compact model is one of the lightest options at roughly 15 pounds. Honestly, it pulls around 95 watts, making it one of the most power-efficient ice makers you can buy. Daily output is about 26 pounds. Now, the tradeoff is that it feels a bit less sturdy than the Euhomy. I wouldn’t toss it around, but if you pack it carefully, it holds up fine. The low weight and low wattage make it a strong pick if you’re running off a smaller power station. ### Frigidaire EFIC108 compact ice maker

Frigidaire’s been in the appliance game forever, and this model reflects that. But it weighs about 21 pounds and draws around 120 watts. Output is about 26 pounds per day, with a first batch in roughly 7 minutes. The build quality feels a step above the budget options. Worth mentioning: it’s not the lightest or most efficient, but it strikes a good balance across everything. I’ve seen these at campsites more than any other model. ### NewAir NIM026SS countertop ice maker

NewAir’s offering weighs around 22 pounds and draws approximately 110 watts. It puts out about 28 pounds of ice per day, which is higher than most in this class. The stainless steel sides give it some extra durability, though they also add a little weight. So if you’re camping with a group and need more ice, the extra output is worth it. First batch lands in about 8 minutes. ### Igloo ICEB26HNSS automatic ice maker

Igloo knows portable cooling, and this ice maker draws about 115 watts with a weight of roughly 21 pounds. Real talk: it produces around 26 pounds per day. What I like about this one for camping is the self-cleaning function. After a few days outdoors, being able to run a cleaning cycle before packing up is genuinely useful. First batch comes in about 7 minutes. OK so it’s a solid middle-of-the-road choice. ## Pairing your ice maker with a portable power station

This is where the camping ice maker setup either works great or falls flat. Real talk: the math isn’t complicated, but you’ve to do it. Take the wattage of your ice maker and compare it to the capacity and output of your power station. An ice maker that draws 100 watts and runs for about 30 minutes per hour (they cycle on and off) uses roughly 50 watt-hours per hour of effective use. Over 8 hours, that’s about 400Wh. Here’s how some popular power stations stack up:

The Jackery Explorer 500 has a 518Wh capacity and 500W output. It’ll run a 100-watt ice maker for most of a day with some room to spare. It’s a good match for the Silonn or Euhomy. The EcoFlow River 2 Max offers 512Wh and 500W output, with a fast recharge if you’ve access to a car or solar panels. Similar performance to the Jackery 500. The Jackery Explorer 1000 at 1002Wh gives you a full weekend of ice-making on a single charge if you’re conservative about when you run it. Real talk: this is the one I personally use. The Bluetti EB55 comes in at 537Wh with a 700W output rating. Now, plenty of headroom for any ice maker on this list, and the extra output capacity means you can run other things at the same time. The EcoFlow Delta 2 at 1024Wh is overkill for just an ice maker, but if you’re powering a whole campsite setup with lights, a fan and phone charging, it handles all of it without breaking a sweat. One thing to keep in mind: the startup surge. When a compressor kicks on, it briefly draws more power than its rated wattage. Most modern power stations handle this fine, but if you’re using a smaller unit near its limits, check that it has surge protection. Honestly, if you’re using your ice maker in an RV instead, the power situation is usually simpler. These aren’t things I read somewhere. They’re things I figured out by doing it wrong first. Pre-chill your water before you leave home. Fill a few bottles and stick them in the fridge overnight. Thing is, cold water makes ice faster, which means less power used. It sounds minor but it genuinely helps when you’re running off a battery. Bring a good cooler to store the ice. The ice maker’s built-in basket is tiny. Make a batch, dump it into a quality cooler, repeat. You can build up a full cooler of ice in a few hours and then turn the machine off for the rest of the day. Look, this saves a ton of power. Thing is, drain the water reservoir completely before transporting. Leftover water will slosh around and can leak out the vents. Ask me how I know. I always tip mine upside down over a drain or the ground, let it sit for a minute, then pack it. Keep the ice maker in the shade. Fun fact: worth mentioning: direct sunlight makes the compressor work harder and produces less ice. Set it under your canopy or on a shaded table. This also helps with power consumption. Give it ventilation space. These machines have exhaust vents, usually on the back or sides. And don’t push it against a wall or pile stuff around it. It needs airflow to work properly, especially in warm weather. Quick note: don’t run it overnight. It’s tempting, but the noise might bother your neighbors, and you’ll drain your power station for ice you don’t need at 3 AM. Make ice in the afternoon, store it in the cooler, and you’ll be set through the next day. Use filtered or clean water. Lake water, creek water, or heavily mineral-laden campground water can leave deposits in the machine and affect ice taste. Worth mentioning: bring jugs of water from home or use a filter. ## When it doesn’t make sense

I’m not going to pretend this is the right move for every camping trip. If you’re doing a quick overnight, it’s not worth hauling the extra gear. A bag of ice from the store handles one night just fine. If you’re backpacking or carrying everything in by hand, forget it. Look, the weight and power requirements make it impossible. And if you’re car camping in extreme heat above 100 degrees, the machine will struggle. They work best when the ambient temperature is below 90. Past that, output drops and power use goes up. Where it really shines is extended car camping trips of three days or more at a site where you either have power or a solid power station. Quick note: that’s when the convenience and cost savings of making your own ice start to add up. No more ice runs, no more soggy cooler drain. Just ice on demand. ## A setup that actually works

My go-to camping ice setup is the Euhomy IM-F, a Jackery Explorer 1000, and a 45-quart hard cooler. I make ice for about two hours in the afternoon, fill the cooler, and turn the machine off. That gives me enough ice for drinks and food storage through the next day, and the power station barely dips below 60%. And if you’re already into tailgating, a lot of this gear pulls double duty. The same ice maker that works at the campsite works great in a parking lot. We covered that in our best ice makers for tailgating guide. The whole portable ice maker for camping thing isn’t for everyone. But if you camp often, already have a power station, and like cold drinks, it’s one of those upgrades you won’t want to give back.
