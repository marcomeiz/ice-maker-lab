Are portable ice makers worth it? (honest take)

2025-05-20 · Guides and tips

I bought my first portable ice maker on a whim two years ago. My fridge’s built-in ice maker had died, and I didn’t feel like paying $200 for a repair. So I grabbed a countertop unit for $89 and figured I’d use it until I got around to fixing the fridge. I never fixed the fridge. So are portable ice makers worth it? Look, for most people, yes. But I’m not going to pretend they’re perfect, and there are specific situations where you’d be throwing money away. I’ve tested five different models over two years, and I’ve a pretty clear picture of who benefits from one and who doesn’t. Now, let me break it all down. ## The short answer

Portable ice makers are worth it for the majority of households that use ice regularly. They pay for themselves within a few months compared to buying bagged ice, they make ice fast, and they sit on your counter without needing any installation. But there are caveats. They don’t store ice like a freezer does. They take up counter space. And if you barely use ice, the math doesn’t work in your favor. I’ll get into all of that below. The people who love these machines really love them. The people who regret buying one usually fall into a pretty predictable category. The trick is figuring out which group you’re in before you spend the money. OK so if you want to skip straight to product recommendations, I put together a list of the 9 best portable ice makers that covers different budgets and needs. ## How much ice they actually make

Marketing claims on portable ice makers are technically accurate but a little misleading. When a box says “26 pounds of ice per day,” that’s true under perfect conditions. But here’s what that looks like in practice. Thing is, most countertop ice makers produce between 26 and 35 pounds of ice per day. That’s roughly 200 to 280 ice cubes, depending on the size setting. The first batch of ice is ready in about 6 to 10 minutes, which is genuinely impressive. You won’t wait long. Here’s the catch: that daily total assumes you’re running the machine continuously and removing ice as it’s made. Since portable ice makers don’t have a freezer compartment, the ice sits in a basket and slowly melts. The melted water recirculates back into the reservoir and gets frozen again, which is efficient but means you’re not accumulating 26 pounds of stored ice. OK so in real-world use, I get about 3 to 4 pounds of ice at any given time sitting in the basket. That’s enough for a family dinner, a round of drinks for guests, or keeping a cooler topped off throughout the day. If you need a huge stockpile all at once, you’ll want to run the machine for a few hours and transfer batches into a freezer bag. Honestly, for a single person or a couple, one machine handles daily ice needs without breaking a sweat. For a family of four or someone who entertains often, you’ll be running it more frequently but it still keeps up. I hosted a backyard barbecue with 15 people last summer and my ice maker handled it just fine. I just started it early in the morning and moved batches to a cooler. And the size of ice matters too. Most machines offer two or three sizes. Small cubes freeze faster, which means higher daily output. Large cubes take a bit longer but melt slower in drinks. I usually run mine on the large setting for cocktails and the small setting when I’m filling a cooler. ## The cost breakdown

This is where portable ice makers start to look really good. A decent portable ice maker costs between $80 and $200. Fun fact: you can find budget models around $80 that work perfectly fine for basic ice needs, and I’ve reviewed several in my guide to the best ice makers under /ice-maker-lab/best-ice-maker-under-100/00. Premium models with nugget ice or larger capacities run $200 to $500, but most people don’t need to spend that much. Now let’s talk operating costs. I was curious about this, so I actually measured it. My portable ice maker uses about 100 to 150 watts when actively freezing. OK so running it for 8 hours a day, which is more than most people would, costs roughly $1 to $3 per month in electricity depending on your local rates. I wrote a deeper breakdown on ice maker electricity costs if you want the exact math. Compare that to buying bagged ice. Here’s the thing: a 10-pound bag costs $3 to $5 at most gas stations and grocery stores. If your household goes through two bags a week, that’s $24 to $40 per month. Over a year, you’re spending $288 to $480 on bagged ice. Here’s the math that sold me:

A $100 portable ice maker plus $2 per month in electricity costs you $124 in the first year. Buying bagged ice at the same consumption rate costs $288 to $480. Honestly, you save $164 to $356 in year one alone. By year two, the savings are even bigger because you’ve already paid for the machine. Honestly, even if you’re a light ice user and only buy one bag a week, a portable ice maker pays for itself in about 6 to 8 months. The only scenario where bagged ice makes more financial sense is if you use ice so rarely that you buy maybe one bag a month. At that point, sure, skip the machine. But if ice is a regular part of your life, the numbers favor owning one. Water cost is negligible, by the way. We’re talking about pennies per day. Now, i’ve seen some people worry about it, but it’s genuinely not a factor. ## Who should absolutely get one

Some people will get way more value from a portable ice maker than others. If you fall into any of these groups, stop debating and just buy one. People who entertain regularly. If you host dinner parties, barbecues, game nights, or any gathering where drinks are involved, a portable ice maker is a game changer. Running to the store for bags of ice before every event gets old fast. Having a machine that churns out ice all day means one less thing to worry about. I’ve started mine before guests arrive and it runs quietly in the background, producing ice all evening. Now, people in hot climates. I live in Texas, and from May through October, ice consumption in my house basically doubles. Cold water, iced coffee, smoothies, keeping coolers stocked for outdoor activities. Here’s the thing: if you live somewhere hot, you’ll use more ice than you think. RV and boat owners. Quick note: portable ice makers were practically designed for this use case. They’re compact, they run on standard 110V outlets, and they don’t need a water line hookup. Just fill the reservoir and plug it in. OK so i know several people who keep a dedicated unit in their RV and swear by it. Anyone whose fridge ice maker broke or doesn’t have one. This was my entry point. If your fridge doesn’t make ice or the ice maker died, a portable unit is cheaper than a repair and arguably more convenient. Fun fact: fridge ice makers are slow. Thing is, portable ones are fast. Nugget ice fans. If you’re obsessed with that soft, chewable nugget ice from Sonic or Chick-fil-A, a nugget ice maker is the only way to get it at home. These machines cost more, usually $200 to $500, but for the people who want that specific ice type, there’s no substitute. It’s worth every penny if that’s your thing. So office and break room use. A portable ice maker in a shared workspace means everyone has access to ice without a plumbed-in solution. Easy to set up, easy to maintain. For a full rundown of the benefits and trade-offs, check out my ice maker pros and cons article. ## Who should skip it

I’m not going to tell everyone to buy one. That wouldn’t be honest. Worth mentioning: here’s who should probably save their money. People who rarely use ice. If you go weeks without thinking about ice, a portable ice maker will collect dust on your counter. Some people just aren’t ice people, and that’s fine. Don’t buy a machine for a habit you don’t have. People with tiny kitchens and no counter space. Real talk: portable ice makers are about the size of a bread machine. That’s not huge, but in a cramped apartment kitchen where every inch counts, it might be one appliance too many. Measure your counter before buying. Most units are roughly 12 by 9 by 13 inches, give or take. People who are happy with their fridge ice maker. If your refrigerator has a working ice maker and it produces enough ice for your needs, you probably don’t need a second source. Here’s the thing: the exception would be if you want a specific ice type, like nugget ice, that your fridge can’t make. Or if your fridge ice tastes weird because you haven’t changed the filter in two years. But that’s a filter problem, not an ice maker problem. People who expect it to replace a freezer. Now, if you want to stockpile ice and pull from a reserve over several days, a portable ice maker alone won’t do that. You’d need to transfer ice to your freezer for storage. The machine itself doesn’t keep ice frozen. It makes ice, holds it temporarily, and the melted water recycles. Here’s the thing: if that workflow sounds annoying to you, it might not be the right fit. ## The downsides nobody talks about

I like my portable ice maker a lot, but I’d be doing you a disservice if I didn’t mention the things that bug me about it. Most review sites gloss over these. It doesn’t store ice. I’ve mentioned this already, but it’s worth repeating because it’s the number one surprise for new owners. The ice basket isn’t a freezer. Ice will melt within a couple of hours if you’re not using it or transferring it. But the machine recycles the water, so nothing is wasted, but you can’t just make a bunch of ice at night and expect it to be there in the morning. If you want a comparison with just keeping trays in the freezer, I covered that in ice maker vs freezer ice trays. Noise. Most portable ice makers produce a low hum when running, similar to a mini fridge. OK so it’s not loud, but it’s not silent either. If you’re sensitive to background noise or your kitchen is open to your living room, you’ll notice it. Some models are quieter than others. In my experience, the noise ranges from barely noticeable to mildly annoying, depending on the brand. OK so i wouldn’t run one in a bedroom. Counter space. It’s another appliance on your counter. If you’re someone who likes a clean, minimalist kitchen, this will bother you. I keep mine out all the time because I use it daily, but I know people who store it in a cabinet and pull it out as needed. That works, but it’s a bit of a hassle. Cleaning is necessary. Look, every two to three weeks, you need to clean a portable ice maker to prevent mineral buildup and funky-tasting ice. So it’s not hard. Usually you just run a cycle with a water and vinegar solution. But you’ve to actually do it. I wrote a step-by-step guide on how to clean your ice maker because a lot of people skip this and then wonder why their ice tastes off after a few months. Water quality matters. If your tap water tastes bad, your ice will taste bad. Portable ice makers don’t have built-in filters (most of them, anyway). Using filtered water makes a noticeable difference. I run water through a Brita pitcher before pouring it into the reservoir. Ice shape limitations. Most portable ice makers produce bullet-shaped ice, those hollow cylinders. They work fine in drinks but they’re not traditional cubes. Worth mentioning: thing is, if you care about ice aesthetics for cocktails, this might bug you. Nugget ice machines are the exception, producing soft, chewable pellets instead. ## Portable vs built-in vs fridge ice maker

You’ve got three main options for ice at home. Here’s how they stack up. Portable ice makers cost $80 to $500, need no installation, sit on your counter, and produce ice in minutes. Honestly, they don’t store ice and require manual water filling. Real talk: they’re the best option for renters, people who don’t want a project, and anyone who needs ice in a non-kitchen location like a patio or RV. Built-in ice makers cost $500 to $3,000 plus installation. They require a water line and a drain, and they’re permanently installed under a counter or in a bar area. They store ice, produce more of it, and look sleek. If you’re building a home bar or remodeling a kitchen, these make sense. Look, for everyone else, they’re overkill and expensive. Fridge ice makers come with your refrigerator, assuming your model has one. They produce ice slowly, maybe 3 to 5 pounds per day, and store it in a bin in your freezer. They’re convenient because they’re already there, but the ice often picks up freezer odors and production can’t keep up with heavy demand. So for most people reading this article, a portable ice maker hits the sweet spot of cost and output. Built-ins are for people with bigger budgets and permanent setups. Fridge ice makers are fine as a supplement but struggle as a primary ice source during high-use periods. ## How long do they last? A fair question, and one that affects the value calculation. In my experience, a well-maintained portable ice maker lasts 2 to 5 years. Budget models tend toward the lower end, while higher-quality units can push past the 5-year mark. And my first $89 machine lasted about 18 months before the compressor started struggling. My current unit, which cost $150, has been going strong for over a year with no issues. The biggest factors affecting lifespan are cleaning frequency and how often you run it. Quick note: hard water areas will see more mineral buildup, which stresses the internal components. Using filtered or distilled water helps. Regular cleaning helps even more. Even at the low end of 2 years, the cost per day of ownership is pretty small. A $100 machine over 2 years is about 14 cents per day. And not exactly a financial burden. If a unit dies, they’re cheap enough to replace without feeling like you just lost a major investment. That’s part of their appeal over a $1,500 built-in unit where a breakdown actually hurts. ## What to look for when buying one

If I’ve convinced you that a portable ice maker is worth it, here are the things I’d pay attention to when shopping. Daily ice production. Look for at least 26 pounds per day. Anything less and you’ll feel like you’re constantly waiting. Ice size options. Two or three size settings give you flexibility. I use different sizes for different situations and it’s nice to have the choice. But reservoir capacity. Larger reservoirs mean less frequent refilling. Look for at least 2 liters. Some models hold 3 or more. Noise level. If the manufacturer lists decibel ratings, compare them. Under 40 dB is quiet enough for most kitchens. Fun fact: self-cleaning function. Some newer models have a self-cleaning cycle built in. It’s a nice convenience, though you should still do a manual deep clean periodically. Brand reputation and warranty. Stick with brands that have decent customer service. A 1 to 2 year warranty is standard. OK so i’ve dealt with warranty claims on two occasions and the process was painless with a reputable brand.
