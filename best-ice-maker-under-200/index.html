Best ice maker under $200 that’s actually good

I’ve spent the last year testing countertop ice makers at basically every price point, and I keep coming back to the same conclusion: the $100-200 range is where things get interesting. Under $100, you’re mostly looking at bullet ice machines that do one thing okay. Once you cross that line, you start seeing nugget ice options, better compressors, stainless steel builds, and machines that don’t sound like a lawnmower at 2am. If you’ve already read my best ice makers under /ice-maker-lab/best-ice-maker-under-100/00 roundup and felt underwhelmed, this is the upgrade you’re looking for. Here’s the thing: i tested seven machines in this bracket over several months, rotating them through my kitchen and my garage tailgate season. Here’s what I found. ## The $200 sweet spot: what you get vs under $100

Let me be straight about what changes when you bump your budget from $80-90 to the $130-170 range. It’s not just a marginal upgrade. It’s a different category of machine. Now, under $100, almost every ice maker produces bullet ice. Those hollow, cylindrical pieces that melt fast and taste vaguely like freezer. The compressors are loud, the water reservoirs are small, and the plastic builds feel like they’ll crack if you look at them wrong. Most of them top out at about 26 pounds of ice per day on paper, but realistically you’re getting maybe 18-20 pounds in a normal kitchen environment. Between $100 and $200, three things change. But first, you get access to nugget ice. That soft, chewable, Sonic-style ice that people genuinely obsess over. The Ecozy and Kndko in this list both make it, and it’s legitimately good. Second, build quality jumps. You’ll see stainless steel panels, better insulation, and quieter operation. Third, daily production goes up to 30-40+ pounds, which means you can actually host a party without running out of ice halfway through. Honestly, the gap between a $90 machine and a $130 machine is bigger than the gap between $130 and $250. So that’s what makes this price range so compelling. ## 1. Ecozy nugget ice maker ($170)

This is the one I reach for most often, and it’s the clear winner of this roundup. The Ecozy makes nugget ice. Worth mentioning: real, soft, chewable nugget ice that’s close to what you’d get from a commercial Scotsman machine. And not identical, but close enough that my wife stopped complaining about our ice situation, which is saying something. Production sits around 33 pounds per day, and a full basket takes roughly 20 minutes to fill up. Build quality is solid for the price. The exterior is a matte plastic that doesn’t show fingerprints, and the interior basket is removable for easy cleaning. Noise level is moderate. You’ll hear it cycling, but it’s not going to wake anyone up from the next room. Who should buy it: anyone who cares about ice type more than ice volume. OK so if you’ve been eyeing a best nugget ice makers but don’t want to spend $400+, this is your entry point. What sucks: the reservoir is only 1.3 liters, so you’re refilling more often than you’d like. And the ice melts faster than bullet ice because of the porous structure, so if you’re not using it quickly, it turns into a wet clump at the bottom of the basket. No self-draining feature either. ## 2. Quick note: newair AI-100 ($130)

The Newair AI-100 is the most balanced machine here. Not the cheapest, not the most exciting, but consistently reliable in a way that a lot of countertop ice makers just aren’t. OK so it produces bullet ice in two sizes and cranks out about 28 pounds per day. A batch of nine pieces takes around 7 minutes, which is fast. The stainless steel exterior looks clean on a countertop and the footprint is surprisingly compact. I’ve got a full Newair ice maker review if you want the deep dive. Build quality impressed me more than the price would suggest. Thing is, the hinged lid feels sturdy, the control panel is simple, and the compressor runs quieter than most machines I’ve tested in this range. It also has a decent insulation layer that keeps finished ice from melting too fast. Who should buy it: someone who wants a reliable daily-use ice maker without any gimmicks. It does bullet ice well and it’s going to last. What sucks: no nugget ice option. Honestly, the large ice setting produces pieces that are still smaller than what a freezer tray makes. And the water reservoir indicator could be more accurate. I ran it dry twice before I learned to just check manually. ## 3. Kndko self-dispensing ($160)

The Kndko has a party trick that actually matters: a self-dispensing chute on the front. But press a button, ice drops into your glass. No scooping, no opening the lid, no wet hands. It sounds like a convenience gimmick until you use it during a gathering and realize how much easier it makes everything. It produces nugget ice at about 30 pounds per day. The nuggets aren’t quite as soft as the Ecozy’s, they’re slightly denser and crunchier, but still firmly in the “good nugget ice” category. The machine is bigger than most on this list, so measure your counter space before buying. Who should buy it: people who entertain regularly or hate scooping ice. So the dispensing mechanism is genuinely useful and holds up well. What sucks: it’s loud. Noticeably louder than every other machine on this list. The compressor has a low hum that’s fine, but the dispensing mechanism adds a grinding sound every time it cycles. But also, at $160, you’re paying a premium for the dispenser feature over what you’d spend on the Ecozy for better ice quality. ## 4. Costway stainless ($150)

If you care about how the machine looks on your counter, the Costway is the most kitchen-friendly option here. Full stainless steel exterior, clean lines, and a build that feels heavier and more permanent than most portable ice makers. It makes bullet ice in two sizes, about 33 pounds per day. And honestly, cycle time is around 8 minutes for a batch, which is competitive. The basket holds roughly 1.5 pounds before you need to empty it, and the insulation is good enough that ice stays solid for a couple of hours even in a warm kitchen. Who should buy it: anyone who wants their ice maker to look like it belongs next to a KitchenAid mixer. The stainless build also makes it a good choice for outdoor bars or man caves where aesthetics matter. What sucks: the drainage plug is in an awkward spot on the back, making it annoying to empty. The control panel uses touch-sensitive buttons that sometimes don’t register on the first press. And for $150, you’re paying a lot for the stainless finish when the Newair at $130 performs about the same with a smaller footprint. ## 5. But silonn countertop pro ($140)

Also good for small kitchens or dorm rooms where counter space is tight. Now, what sucks: the ice basket is small. You’ll be emptying it frequently if you’re making ice for more than one person. The plastic build doesn’t feel as sturdy as the Newair or Costway, and the lid has a slight wobble that bugs me every time I open it. Minor stuff, but at $140, I expect cleaner fit and finish. ## 6. And if you’ve got the budget for it, the best nugget ice makers roundup goes into the higher-end nugget options that compete with commercial machines. ## What to actually look for in this price range

Before you buy, here are the things that actually matter when you’re spending $100-200 on a countertop ice maker. Ice type is the biggest decision. Bullet ice is fine for drinks. Nugget ice is better for chewing and smoothies. They’re different products for different people. Here’s the thing: decide which one you want before you start comparing specs. Daily production numbers are optimistic. Now, every manufacturer tests in ideal conditions. In a real kitchen, expect about 70-80% of the listed output. A machine rated at 33 pounds per day will realistically give you 23-26 pounds. Plan accordingly if you’re buying for parties or heavy use. Noise matters more than you think. So these machines run compressor cycles every 7-10 minutes when they’re producing. If it’s going on your kitchen counter, you’ll hear it during conversations and while watching TV. Worth mentioning: i measured everything from 38 dB to 52 dB across these seven machines. That range is the difference between a quiet library and a normal conversation. Self-cleaning functions save real time. Worth mentioning: the machines that have them (Silonn, Ecozy) are noticeably easier to maintain. Without it, you’re doing a vinegar rinse every week or two, which isn’t hard but is annoying enough that most people skip it until the ice starts tasting weird. Reservoir size determines how often you babysit the machine. Anything under 2 liters means you’re refilling multiple times per day during heavy use. None of these machines connect to a water line, so this is a real consideration. OK so spend your money on ice quality and reliability. Skip the flashy features if they come at the expense of either one. A machine that makes great ice quietly for three years beats a feature-packed machine that breaks in eight months every single time.
