Best ice maker for tailgating and parties

Last football season, I brought a portable ice maker to the parking lot for the first time. OK so by halftime of the pre-game setup, every neighboring tailgate was asking where I got it. We had 15 people rotating through our spot, and we never ran out of ice. Not once. Meanwhile, the group two trucks over was rationing melted convenience store ice from a bag they bought at 7 AM. If you’re serious about tailgating, or you throw backyard parties, cookouts, or any gathering where drinks need to stay cold, a portable ice maker is one of the best things you can add to your setup. But you need the right one. Tailgating isn’t the same as sitting on a kitchen counter. You need speed, portability, a solid power solution, and something that can take a bump without falling apart. Here’s what to look for and my four favorite picks. ## Speed is everything

At a tailgate or party, you’re not making ice for two people sipping water. You’re keeping a cooler stocked for a crowd. The difference between a machine that makes 26 pounds per day and one that makes 40-plus pounds per day is the difference between keeping up and falling behind. Most portable ice makers produce their first batch in 6 to 10 minutes, with a full basket (holding about 1.5 to 2 pounds) ready every 15 to 20 minutes after that. At a busy tailgate, you’ll dump that basket into a cooler and the machine starts over immediately. Quick note: a 40-pound-per-day machine cycling all morning can fill a 48-quart cooler before kickoff. The trick is starting early. Set up the ice maker as soon as you arrive and let it run continuously. Don’t wait until people show up asking for ice. Front-load your production and dump every batch into the cooler. ## Powering your ice maker in a parking lot

This is the question that stops most people. You’re in a stadium parking lot or a park. There’s no outlet. So how do you run an appliance that needs 100 to 150 watts? A portable power station is the cleanest option. Something like a Jackery 300 or EcoFlow River 2 gives you enough capacity to run a portable ice maker for 3 to 5 hours on a single charge. That’s plenty for a tailgate. These power stations are quiet, charge from your car on the drive over, and can run other things too. If you want to know the exact draw, I’ve a detailed breakdown of [how much electricity portable ice makers use]ice maker electricity costs. A generator works if you already have one, but generators are loud, need fuel, and some tailgate venues don’t allow them. A small 1000-watt inverter generator handles an ice maker easily, but check the parking lot rules first. Your car’s 12V outlet with an inverter is a backup option. Run the car for a while to keep the battery charged. It works in a pinch but it’s not ideal for extended tailgates because you’re putting hours of load on your car battery. Some newer tailgate setups use solar panels feeding a battery bank. It’s overkill for just an ice maker, but if you’ve already got a solar and battery setup for other gear, adding an ice maker to the load is simple. ## Portability and durability

You’re carrying this thing across a parking lot, setting it on a folding table, and using it around people who aren’t being especially careful. It needs to be light enough to carry with one hand (or at least have a good handle), and it needs to handle the occasional bump. Weight matters. Most 26-pound-per-day models weigh between 14 and 20 pounds. The bigger 40-plus-pound models can hit 30 pounds. That’s manageable but you’ll want a handle. Build quality matters too. A machine that rattles when you set it down hard probably won’t last a full season. Look for solid latches on the lid and a sturdy base that doesn’t leak when tilted slightly. ## My 4 picks for tailgating ice makers

Run the machine on a stable, level surface. Folding tables work but make sure the legs are locked and the table is on solid ground. An ice maker on an uneven surface won’t drain properly and the ice quality suffers. Bring extra water. These machines are manual-fill, meaning you pour water into the top. A tailgate uses a lot of ice, and that ice comes from water you’ve to supply. Bring two or three extra gallons beyond what you’d normally carry for drinking. You’ll go through it. Keep the ice maker in the shade if possible. Thing is, under a pop-up canopy is ideal. Direct sun on a hot September parking lot makes the compressor struggle, slows production, and the ice melts faster in the basket before you can get it to the cooler. Dump ice into the cooler frequently. Don’t wait for the basket to overflow. Every 15 to 20 minutes, grab the basket, dump it into your main cooler, and put the basket back. This keeps the machine cycling at maximum speed because it works most efficiently when the basket isn’t full. Pre-chill your cooler. If you put ice into a hot cooler, half of it melts just bringing the cooler down to temperature. The night before, throw a bag of ice in there and let it sit. OK so in the morning, drain the melt water and start adding fresh machine-made ice to an already-cold interior. ## Pairing with a camping setup

A portable power station that runs your ice maker at the stadium works just as well at a campsite. ## Final word

A portable ice maker at a tailgate is one of those things that sounds unnecessary until you see it in action. Once you’ve got a constant supply of fresh ice for five or six hours straight, going back to the “buy bags and hope they last” approach feels primitive. Any of the four machines above will handle a tailgate crowd. Now, pick based on your group size, your power setup, and how much you want to carry. And start making ice the second you park. Your future self will thank you.
