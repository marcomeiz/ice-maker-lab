GE profile opal nugget ice maker review: 6 months of daily use

I spent $579 on an ice maker. Look, not a freezer, not a fridge. An ice maker. If you told me two years ago I’d willingly drop that kind of money on a countertop appliance that does one thing, I’d’ve laughed. But here I’m, six months in, still using my GE Profile Opal 2.0 every single day. And I don’t regret it. That said, this machine isn’t for everyone. It has real flaws. The price is steep. And there’s a very specific type of person who should buy this. So let me break down everything I’ve learned after half a year of ownership. ## What makes nugget ice different

If you’ve ever had ice from Sonic Drive-In, Chick-fil-A, or a high-end hospital (weirdly, hospitals have great ice), you already know what nugget ice is. It’s soft and porous. It soaks up whatever drink it’s sitting in. Regular ice just floats there and waters things down. Nugget ice becomes part of the drink. That’s the entire reason the Opal exists. Real talk: it makes that specific type of ice at home. No other countertop machine produces nugget ice at this quality level. I’ve looked into every option on the best nugget ice makers list, and the Opal still sits at the top. Bullet ice makers cost a fraction of the price, but they make a completely different product. Comparing the two is like comparing a toaster to an oven. They both apply heat, but the results aren’t the same. The ice comes out in small, irregularly shaped pieces that crunch perfectly. Honestly, it’s not hard on your teeth. And you can chew through a whole glass without thinking about it. My wife, who never cared about ice before, now refuses to use regular ice cubes. That should tell you something. ## Production speed and capacity

GE claims the Opal produces about 1 pound of ice per hour. In my experience, that’s accurate once the machine is fully running, which takes roughly 20 minutes from startup. Look, the internal bin holds about 3 pounds, so you’re looking at a full bin in roughly three hours. For a household of two, that’s plenty. We run it in the morning and have ice all day. Quick note: if you’re hosting a party or have a bigger family, you’ll want to use the side tank. The Opal 2.0 comes with a side tank that attaches to the machine and adds extra water capacity. Without it, you’re refilling the reservoir constantly. Now, with it, the machine can run much longer before needing attention. I leave the side tank attached permanently. There’s no good reason to take it off unless you’re tight on counter space. Speaking of counter space, this thing is bigger than most portable ice makers. Quick note: it measures roughly 15.5 inches tall, 10.5 inches wide, and 17.5 inches deep (with the side tank). Quick note: if you’re used to those compact bullet ice makers that tuck neatly into a corner, the Opal will feel like a serious commitment. I had to rearrange my kitchen counter to make room. It sits between the coffee maker and the stand mixer now, and it earns its spot. ## The app: bluetooth and wifi

The Opal 2.0 connects to the SmartHQ app through both Bluetooth and WiFi. I’ll be honest: I had low expectations. Appliance apps are usually terrible, and the original Opal was notorious for connectivity issues. So the 2.0 version is better, but “better” is relative. Initial setup took me three tries. The Bluetooth connection drops if you walk more than 15 feet from the machine. WiFi is more stable, but there’s a noticeable lag between tapping a button and the machine responding. What can the app actually do? You can start and stop ice production, schedule it to turn on at specific times, adjust ice size (slightly softer or harder nuggets), and trigger the self-cleaning cycle. The scheduling feature is genuinely useful. I’ve mine set to start at 7 AM every morning so there’s ice ready when I come downstairs. Thing is, that alone makes the app worth installing. Everything else feels unnecessary. I don’t need my phone to tell me the bin is full when I can just look at it. The ice firmness adjustment makes a barely noticeable difference. And the app occasionally forgets the machine exists and needs to be re-paired. It’s not a dealbreaker, but calling it a selling point feels generous. ## Noise level

Here’s where I’ve to be straight with you. The Opal is loud. Not “running dishwasher” loud, but definitely louder than any bullet ice maker I’ve owned. There’s a constant hum from the compressor, and when ice drops into the bin, you hear it. Real talk: if your kitchen is open to your living room and you’re watching TV, you’ll notice it. And i measured it at about 55 decibels from three feet away during active production. That’s somewhere between a normal conversation and a running vacuum. It’s not unbearable, but if you were picturing this thing quietly making ice in the background while you sleep, think again. After six months, I’ve tuned it out. My brain treats it like white noise now. But guests always comment on it. “What’s that sound?” is a question I answer at least once per gathering. ## Self-Cleaning

The Opal has a self-cleaning mode that you’re supposed to run every week or two. You add a cleaning solution (or a vinegar-water mix), press the clean button through the app or on the machine itself, and it circulates the solution for about 30 minutes. It works fine. Not much else to say. I run it every Sunday evening, and the machine has stayed fresh. The water filter in the side tank also needs replacing every three months or so. But gE sells them for around $15 each. It’s a minor ongoing cost but worth noting. ## The price discussion

Let’s talk about the elephant on the counter. The GE Profile Opal 2.0 retails for around $579, and I’ve seen it fluctuate between $499 and $629 depending on the retailer and time of year. And that’s a lot of money for ice. For comparison, a decent bullet ice maker costs $70 to $120. You can check out our 9 best portable ice makers roundup for options in that range. They’ll give you ice in minutes, they’re smaller and quieter’t make your wallet cry. But they won’t give you nugget ice. And that distinction matters to a certain kind of person. If you’re someone who buys bags of Sonic ice from the gas station, who orders extra ice at restaurants because you love chewing it, who thinks the ice is the best part of a fountain drink, the Opal pays for itself in happiness. Look, i’m not being dramatic. I was buying two or three bags of nugget ice per week before I got this machine, at $3 to $4 per bag. So the math actually works out in my favor over time. If you just want cold drinks, this is an absurd purchase. Get a 9 best portable ice makers option for under $100 and move on with your life. For cocktail enthusiasts, nugget ice is a mixed bag. It’s perfect for juleps and mojitos-ice drinks where dilution is part of the experience. It’s not great for spirit-forward cocktails where you want slow-melting cubes. I wrote more about this in the best ice makers for cocktails guide if you’re shopping specifically for that use case. ## Common complaints (and my experience)

Reading online reviews, three issues come up constantly: leaking and app connectivity problems. Now, i’ve already covered the noise. Here’s my take on the other two. Leaking seems to be a quality control issue. Some units have it, some don’t. Mine has never leaked, but I’ve read enough reports to believe it’s a real problem for a percentage of buyers. GE’s warranty covers it, and their customer service has been decent when I’ve contacted them about unrelated questions. If you get a leaker, push for a replacement. App connectivity I’ve already discussed. OK so it’s finicky but functional. Here’s the thing: my advice: set up your schedule once and then mostly forget the app exists. The physical controls on the machine work fine for daily use. One complaint I don’t see mentioned enough is how often you need to empty the drip tray. The Opal produces meltwater that collects in a small tray at the back. In humid weather, I’m emptying it daily. Look, it’s a small annoyance, but it’s there. ## What i love

The ice. Full stop. Nothing else on the market makes nugget ice this good at home. I’ve tried the Frigidaire nugget maker and a couple of off-brand options. OK so the Opal produces ice that’s almost indistinguishable from commercial machines. Now, the texture is right. The chewability is right. The way it absorbs flavor from your drink is right. I also appreciate that it looks decent on a counter. It’s not beautiful, but it’s not an eyesore either. Honestly, the stainless steel front fits in with most kitchen aesthetics. GE clearly put thought into the design, which is more than I can say for most portable ice makers. Fun fact: the scheduling feature through the app, despite my complaints about connectivity, has changed my morning routine. Waking up to a full bin of nugget ice for my coffee and water is a small luxury that I now take for granted. If you’re comparing this specifically against other GE models, I put together a breakdown at Opal vs GE Profile comparison that covers the differences in detail. ## The verdict

The GE Profile Opal 2.0 is the best nugget ice maker you can buy for home use. That’s not really debatable right now. Honestly, nothing else matches the ice quality, and the 2.0 version fixed enough of the original’s problems to make it a reliable daily machine. But “best nugget ice maker” only matters if you actually want nugget ice badly enough to spend over $500 on it. If you’re a casual ice user who just wants cold drinks, this is a terrible value. An $80 bullet maker will serve you perfectly well. If you’re the person who gets excited about chewable ice, who notices the difference between good ice and bad ice, who considers ice a feature of the drink rather than an afterthought, the Opal is worth every penny. Real talk: i said what I said. Six months in, I’d buy it again. Worth mentioning: my only wish is that it were quieter and about $150 cheaper. But the ice is genuinely that good, and nothing else comes close.
