Let’s be honest about what you’re giving up by staying in the budget range. These trade-offs are real, and you should know about them before you buy. No nugget ice. Every single machine on this list makes bullet-shaped ice with hollow centers. But if you want those soft, chewable nuggets like you get at Sonic or Chick-fil-A, you’re looking at $150 minimum, and more realistically $200 or higher. Bullet ice is perfectly fine for most drinks, but it’s not the same experience. Not even close. Quick note: plastic builds. Every machine here is primarily plastic construction. The housings, the baskets, the scoops, the internal components. Some feel more solid than others, but none of them feel like premium appliances. You won’t mistake any of these for a built-in ice maker. They feel exactly like what they’re: budget countertop gadgets. No insulation or cooling in the ice basket. This is the one that trips people up the most. These machines make ice, but they don’t keep it frozen. The ice sits in an uninsulated basket, and within an hour it starts melting. But the meltwater drains back into the reservoir and gets recycled into new ice, which is smart. But if you want ice that stays frozen, you’ll need to move it to your freezer or an insulated container. None of these machines have a built-in freezer function. That feature starts showing up in models around $150 to $200. Smaller reservoirs. Most of these hold about two to 2.5 liters of water. That’s enough for several batches, but if you’re making ice all day for a party, you’re refilling every couple of hours. Look, higher-end models often have larger tanks. Shorter lifespans. Look, i won’t sugarcoat it. A $75 to $99 countertop ice maker isn’t going to last you a decade. Most people report getting one to three years out of these machines with regular use. That’s a genuinely good deal. ## Quick tips for getting the most out of your budget ice maker

Start with cold water. Room temperature water works, but if you fill the reservoir with cold water from the fridge, your first batch will be ready a couple of minutes faster. Over the course of a day, that adds up. Run the self-clean cycle weekly. So if your model has one, use it. If it doesn’t, run a cycle with a water and vinegar mix every week or two. Mineral buildup is what kills these machines early. Don’t leave stale water sitting in the reservoir. Thing is, if you’re not going to use the machine for a few days, drain it and leave the lid open so it dries out. Stagnant water leads to funky tastes and potential mold. Transfer your ice to the freezer. Make a batch, dump it in a freezer bag, and repeat. This is the most efficient way to use these machines since they can’t keep ice frozen on their own. ## Final thoughts
