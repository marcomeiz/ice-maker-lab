Best ice maker for RV and van life

If you’ve ever opened your RV freezer expecting ice and found a solid block of frost fused to a half-empty tray, you already know the problem. RV refrigerators weren’t built for ice production. They’re slow and inconsistent compartment barely has room for a bag of frozen peas, let alone a proper ice supply. That’s exactly why a portable ice maker for RV use has become one of the most popular upgrades among full-timers and weekend warriors alike. I spent two summers in a Class C with a Dometic fridge that could maybe produce eight usable cubes in a full day. And on a hot afternoon in Arizona, that’s a joke. A countertop ice maker changed everything. But not every model works well in an RV. You’ve got space limits and power constraints your kitchen shakes down the highway at 60 mph. Let’s talk about what actually works. ## Why RV fridges fail at making ice

Most RV fridges run on absorption cooling. They’re designed to keep food cold, not to freeze water quickly. The freezer section is usually small, sometimes just a metal box tucked inside the fridge cavity. Quick note: temperature fluctuates a lot, especially when the rig is parked in direct sun or when you switch between propane and electric. The result? Fun fact: ice trays take forever. You get cloudy, oddly shaped cubes that taste like whatever else is in the freezer. And if you’re boondocking and running on propane, the freezer performance drops even more. So a portable countertop ice maker solves this by doing one thing well. It makes ice, fast, without relying on your fridge’s freezer. Worth mentioning: most units produce the first batch in under ten minutes. That’s a game changer when you pull into camp after a long drive and just want a cold drink. ## Power: the biggest question for RV ice makers

Before you buy anything, you need to think about how you’ll power it. This matters more in an RV than it does at home. So if you want a deeper look at energy draw, check out our guide on ice maker electricity costs. Shore power is the easiest scenario. Plugged into a 30 or 50 amp hookup at a campground, you can run pretty much any countertop ice maker without thinking twice. Most units draw between 100 and 150 watts while actively freezing, which is nothing compared to your AC unit. Inverter power is where things get trickier. Honestly, if you’re boondocking or dry camping, you’re pulling from your house batteries through an inverter. A 120W ice maker running for a few hours can drain a small battery bank faster than you’d expect. Here’s the thing: you want the lowest wattage unit you can find, and you want to run it strategically. Make a big batch, store it in a cooler, turn the machine off. Generator power works fine, but running a generator just to make ice feels wasteful. Most people use generator time to charge batteries and run the AC, and they’ll toss the ice maker on during that window. So the bottom line: wattage matters. A 100W unit is a lot more practical off-grid than a 180W unit. Check the specs before you buy. ## Size constraints: counter space is gold

RV counters are small. In a van, they’re practically nonexistent. You need a machine with a compact footprint that still produces enough ice to be useful. Look, most portable ice makers are around 12 to 14 inches wide, 10 to 12 inches deep, and 12 to 14 inches tall. That’s roughly the size of a large bread box. Some are smaller. A few are bigger and not worth considering for RV use. Think about where you’ll put it. Fun fact: next to the sink is common since you need to fill the reservoir and drain meltwater. Look, some folks store the machine in a cabinet while driving and pull it out at camp. Others leave it on the counter full-time but secure it for travel. Also consider the reservoir size. A bigger tank means fewer refills, but it also means more weight when full. A 2-liter tank is plenty for two people. Honestly, bigger groups might want a 2.5 or 3-liter capacity. ## Vibration and transport: will it survive the road? This is the one thing most ice maker reviews never talk about, and it’s the most important factor for RV use. These machines have compressors and water pumps. Banging down a washboard dirt road isn’t great for any of them. A few rules to follow. Honestly, always drain the reservoir before driving. Water sloshing around inside can damage the pump and make a mess. Let the machine sit for at least 30 minutes after turning it off before you move it, so the refrigerant settles. And secure it. Bungee cords, a non-slip mat, or a dedicated spot in a cabinet. So you don’t want 20 pounds of ice maker sliding across your counter in a hard brake. Look, some units hold up better than others. Machines with solid, compact builds and fewer external protrusions tend to handle road life better. Avoid anything with a flimsy lid or a reservoir that’s hard to fully drain. ## 5 ice makers that work great in an RV

A solid starter pick if you don’t want to spend over $100. ## Tips for running an ice maker in your RV

A few things I’ve learned the hard way over the years. Quick note: pre-chill your water. Real talk: if you fill the reservoir with water that’s already cold from the fridge, the machine works less hard and produces ice faster. This saves power, which matters when you’re off-grid. Even cool tap water makes a noticeable difference compared to warm stuff straight from a campground spigot on a July afternoon. Pair it with a good cooler. Don’t rely on the ice maker’s built-in storage bin. But those bins aren’t insulated. Ice melts fast, recycles back into the reservoir, and the machine just keeps running in a loop. Make a batch, move it to an insulated cooler or your fridge’s freezer, then make another. You’ll end up with a real stockpile and the machine can rest. Drain it completely before you drive. Real talk: i can’t stress this enough. Tip the machine, let every drop come out, and leave the lid open for a bit to dry. OK so water left inside sloshes, can grow mold in the lines, and might damage the pump over time. It takes two minutes and saves you headaches. Secure it while moving. Here’s the thing: a non-slip shelf liner under the machine works well on its own for smooth highways. For rougher roads, add a bungee cord or a tension rod in front of it. Some people build a small wooden cradle that the machine sits in. Honestly, whatever method you choose, make sure the machine can’t slide or tip. Gravity does weird things on mountain switchbacks. Run it during generator or charging time. Quick note: if you’re boondocking with solar, run the ice maker during peak sun hours when your panels are putting out maximum power. Make all the ice you’ll need and shut the machine down. Quick note: there’s no reason to run it 24/7. Clean it regularly. Campground water quality varies wildly. Some spots have hard water that’ll leave mineral buildup in the machine within a week. Run the self-clean cycle if your model has one. If it doesn’t, a mix of water and white vinegar through the system once a week keeps things fresh. ## Ice makers work on boats too

If you spend time on the water, the same principles apply. Compact size and low wattage handle some motion. Quick note: we put together a separate guide on picking an best ice makers for boats that covers marine-specific concerns like saltwater corrosion and 12V power systems. ## Final thoughts

A portable ice maker is one of those RV upgrades that sounds like a luxury until you use one. And then it feels like a necessity. The key is picking a unit that fits your rig, runs on your available power, and can handle the road without falling apart. For most RVers, something in the 100 to 120 watt range with a footprint under 13 inches wide is the sweet spot. You don’t need the biggest or fanciest model. Real talk: you need one that works reliably, makes ice fast, and doesn’t hog your counter or your battery bank. Stop fighting with your RV freezer. Get a machine that actually does the job.
