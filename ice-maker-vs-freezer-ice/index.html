Portable ice maker vs freezer ice trays

2025-05-20 · Guides and tips

Portable ice maker vs freezer ice trays: which one makes sense for you? now, you’ve been using ice trays your whole life. Look, they work. they’re free. so why would you spend $80 to $150 on a countertop machine that does the same thing? that’s a fair question, and the honest answer is: it depends on how much ice you actually go through. for some people, a portable ice maker is a game changer. thing is, for others, it’s just another appliance collecting dust. Thing is, let’s break down where each option wins and loses so you can figure out which camp you’re in. ## how fast do you need ice? this is where the gap between the two options is massive. a standard ice tray takes 3 to 4 hours to freeze a batch. worth mentioning: if you forgot to refill them last night, you’re out of luck for this afternoon’s drinks. most people have maybe two trays in their freezer, which gives you around 28 to 32 cubes total. Fun fact: once those are gone, you’re waiting half the day for more. a portable ice maker pumps out its first batch in 6 to 10 minutes. not hours. minutes. most machines can produce around 26 pounds of ice per day if you keep feeding them water. Here’s the thing: fun fact: that’s roughly the equivalent of filling and freezing ice trays 15 to 20 times over. if you’re hosting a barbecue, throwing a party, or just have a household that burns through ice on hot summer days, the speed difference alone can justify the purchase. if you’re someone who drops two cubes in a glass of water once a day, speed probably doesn’t matter much to you. ## ice quality and shape

Here’s a spot where ice trays actually hold their own. Freezer ice is solid, dense, and melts slowly. Those classic rectangular cubes do a great job keeping drinks cold without watering them down too quickly. So you can also buy specialty trays that make large spheres, thin sticks for water bottles, or novelty shapes if that’s your thing. Portable ice makers produce bullet-shaped or nugget-style ice. It’s hollow in the middle, which means it melts faster than traditional cubes. For cocktails and sipping drinks, some people find this annoying because their drink gets diluted quicker. So for smoothies, iced coffee, or just keeping a water bottle cold, it works perfectly fine. Neither option produces the crystal-clear ice you see at fancy bars. That requires a different process entirely. But if ice shape and density matter to you, trays give you more control over the final product. ## The real cost breakdown

Ice trays cost a few bucks. Worth mentioning: maybe $5 for a two-pack that’ll last years. But the ongoing cost is essentially zero since your freezer is already running whether there’s ice in it or not. A portable ice maker runs between $80 and $200 for most popular models. You can find solid options if you look at best ice makers under /ice-maker-lab/best-ice-maker-under-100/00. On top of the purchase price, you’re paying for electricity to run it. Most countertop ice makers use between 100 and 150 watts, which translates to roughly $2 to $5 per month if you’re running it daily. Check out our breakdown on ice maker electricity costs for specific numbers. There’s also maintenance. You’ll need to clean the machine every couple of weeks to prevent mineral buildup and keep the ice tasting fresh. It’s not a big deal, but it’s something you never have to think about with trays. Over a year of daily use, a portable ice maker might cost you $100 to $230 all in (purchase plus electricity). Real talk: ice trays cost you $5. Look, over five years, the ice maker could run $120 to $350 total (adding electricity and maybe a replacement unit), while trays are still sitting at $5. If you’re purely looking at cost efficiency, trays win by a mile. But cost efficiency isn’t the only thing that matters, which brings us to convenience. ## Convenience and daily life

This is where the portable ice maker pulls ahead for busy households. With trays, you’ve to remember to fill them. You’ve to make sure they’re level when you put them back. You’ve to crack the ice out without sending cubes flying across the kitchen. And if someone in your house uses the last of the ice without refilling the trays, you’re stuck. OK so with a machine, you add water and press a button. Here’s the thing: that’s it. Ice shows up in minutes, and it keeps making more until the basket is full or you run out of water. There’s no planning ahead, no hoping someone else did their part. For families, people who work from home and drink iced beverages all day, or anyone who entertains regularly, this convenience factor is real. It’s the same reason people buy dishwashers even though hand-washing dishes is technically free. ## Freezer space vs counter space

This is a genuine trade-off worth thinking about. Ice trays live in your freezer. And if you’ve got a packed freezer (and most people do), those two trays are taking up space that could hold actual food. It doesn’t sound like much, but in a small apartment freezer, every inch counts. Now, a portable ice maker sits on your counter. Most models are about the size of a bread box, roughly 14 inches wide, 10 inches deep, and 12 inches tall. If you’ve got a small kitchen, that’s real estate you might not want to give up. You free up freezer space but lose counter space. Honestly, for people with roomy kitchens and cramped freezers, the trade works in the ice maker’s favor. For people with tiny kitchens and plenty of freezer room, trays make more sense. Worth mentioning: we cover this and other trade-offs in our ice maker pros and cons guide. ## Noise and power

Ice trays are silent. They sit in your freezer and do their thing without making a sound. They don’t need to be plugged in. They don’t need their own outlet. Quick note: they just exist. Portable ice makers have a small compressor and fan, similar to a mini fridge. Thing is, most run at about 35 to 45 decibels, which is roughly the level of a quiet conversation or a humming refrigerator. It’s not loud, but it’s not silent either. If you’re putting one in a studio apartment or a bedroom setup, you’ll notice it. They also need a power outlet, and you’ll want to keep them on a flat, stable surface away from heat sources. None of this is a dealbreaker, but it’s a consideration that ice trays don’t have at all. ## What about bags of ice from the store? Worth mentioning since this is the third option most people consider. A bag of ice from a gas station or grocery store runs $2 to $4 and gives you about 10 pounds. If you only need ice for the occasional party, buying a bag is cheaper and easier than owning a machine. But if you’re buying bags weekly, that’s $8 to $16 a month, or $100 to $200 a year. At that point, a portable ice maker starts to pay for itself within the first year, and you never have to make a special trip to the store. ## Who should get a portable ice maker

You go through more than two trays of ice a day on a regular basis. Your freezer is packed and you’re tired of dedicating space to trays. OK so you host people at your house frequently. You hate the routine of filling, freezing, cracking, and refilling. You want ice available on demand without planning ahead. If any of those describe you, a portable ice maker is probably worth the money. But we wrote a full breakdown on are portable ice makers worth it that goes deeper into the value question. ## Who should stick with ice trays

You use ice a few times a week or less. Your kitchen counter space is limited. You don’t want another appliance to maintain or plug in. You’re happy with the ice you’re getting now. You’d rather spend $80 on literally anything else. There’s nothing wrong with ice trays. Quick note: they’ve worked for decades, and they’ll keep working. Not everything needs an upgrade. ## The bottom line

If you’re going through more than two trays of ice per day, a portable ice maker will save you time, free up freezer space, and make your life a little easier. The cost adds up over years, but the convenience is hard to beat for heavy ice users. If you grab a handful of cubes a few times a week, ice trays are the smarter pick. They cost almost nothing, take up no counter space, and require zero maintenance. Worth mentioning: spending $100 on a machine you’ll use twice a week doesn’t make financial sense. Quick note: the right answer isn’t about which option is better in general. It’s about how much ice you personally use and whether the convenience is worth the price tag for your specific situation.
