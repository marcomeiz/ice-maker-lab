Best ice maker for a small kitchen

I live in an apartment with about four feet of usable counter space. Between the coffee maker, the toaster, and the knife block, there’s barely room for a cutting board. So when I started looking for a portable ice maker, size was the only thing that mattered. OK so i didn’t care about production speed or ice type until I knew the machine would actually fit. If you’re dealing with a small kitchen, you already know the struggle. Every appliance has to earn its spot. I spent weeks measuring, comparing, and testing compact ice makers to find the ones that genuinely work in tight spaces. Here are my picks. ## What to look for in a compact ice maker

Before the specific recommendations, a few things to keep in mind. Footprint matters more than height. A machine that’s 9 inches wide and 12 inches deep takes up the same counter real estate whether it’s 10 inches tall or 14 inches tall. So focus on the width and depth first. Height only matters if you’re tucking it under a cabinet. Weight determines whether you’ll actually store it when it’s not in use. If a machine weighs 15 pounds, you’ll probably leave it on the counter permanently. If it’s under 20 pounds, it’s reasonable to move to a closet or pantry between uses. Above that, it’s staying put. Cord length is something nobody thinks about until the machine is on the counter and the cord doesn’t reach the outlet. Most portable ice makers come with 4 to 6 foot cords. Measure your outlet distance before you buy. And don’t forget ventilation. But these machines expel heat from the back or sides. You can’t push them flush against a wall or sandwich them between other appliances without risking overheating. Leave at least 4 to 6 inches of clearance around the vents. ## My top picks for small kitchens

If your kitchen is really tight, using the machine daily and leaving it out might not work. But here’s what I do. I keep my ice maker on a shelf in the hall closet when I’m not using it. When I need ice, I bring it out, fill it, run a few batches, bag the ice, and put it away. The whole process takes about an hour and gives me enough ice for several days. Any machine under 17 pounds is easy to move this way. Above that, it starts to feel like a chore. If you’re planning to store and retrieve the machine regularly, weight should be a top priority. Another option is a rolling kitchen cart. Here’s the thing: a small cart that tucks against a wall gives you a dedicated ice maker station that doesn’t use counter space. Roll it out when you need it, roll it back when you’re done. I’ve seen this setup work really well in studio apartments and tiny kitchens. ## How these compare to full-size models

For a small kitchen, the production difference isn’t worth the space trade-off. Twenty-six pounds is plenty for most households. Look, that’s roughly enough to fill a standard cooler.
