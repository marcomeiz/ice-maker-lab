Silonn ice maker review: worth the hype?

2025-05-20 · Reviews

Silonn ice maker review: worth the hype? honestly, i bought the silonn countertop ice maker about three months ago because i got tired of filling ice trays like it was 1997. the thing sits at the top of amazon’s best-seller list with over 40,000 reviews, and i wanted to know if those ratings were legit. so here’s my honest silonn ice maker review after using it pretty much every day since it showed up at my door. short answer: it’s solid for what it costs. long answer: keep reading. ## what you get for around $100

The Silonn is a countertop bullet ice maker that runs about $90 to $110 depending on the day and whatever color you pick. Look, mine is the silver one. It makes bullet-shaped ice in two sizes (small and large), holds about 2 liters of water in its reservoir, and claims to pop out 9 ice cubes every 6 minutes. Worth mentioning: it weighs around 14 pounds and takes up roughly the same counter space as a large toaster. Not tiny, but not a beast either. I keep mine next to the coffee maker and it fits fine in my apartment kitchen. Honestly, inside the box you get the machine, a small ice scoop, and the manual. That’s it. No water line, no drain hose, nothing complicated. ## Setting it up

This was the easiest appliance I’ve ever set up. Worth mentioning: i’m not exaggerating. You take it out of the box, peel off the protective film, set it on the counter, and pour water into the reservoir. Then you press the power button, pick your ice size, and wait. The first batch came out in about 8 minutes. I timed it. No plumbing. Honestly, no installation. Here’s the thing: no YouTube tutorial needed. I had ice in my glass within 10 minutes of opening the box. One thing the manual says is to run the self-cleaning cycle before your first use and to dump the first two batches of ice. I did that. Took maybe 20 minutes total before I had “real” ice I could actually use. ## Ice production speed

Silonn says 9 cubes in 6 minutes. In my testing, the first cycle usually takes closer to 8 minutes. Real talk: after the machine has been running for 20 to 30 minutes and the internal components are cold, it does hit that 6-minute mark pretty consistently. Over a full day, the machine can make about 26 pounds of ice according to the spec sheet. I’ve never needed that much, but during a small get-together with 8 people, I ran it continuously for about 4 hours and it kept up just fine. We weren’t rationing ice or anything. Here’s the thing: the ice basket holds roughly 1.5 to 2 pounds of ice at a time. When the basket is full, the machine stops automatically and restarts when you scoop some out. That sensor works well. Quick note: never had it overflow. ## Ice quality

Here’s where you need to manage your expectations. The Silonn makes bullet ice. Fun fact: these are hollow, cylindrical pieces that look like little thimbles. They’re not nugget ice. They’re not the crunchy, chewable stuff you get at Sonic or Chick-fil-A. If you’re buying this specifically because you want nugget ice, stop right now and look at a different machine. I’ve written about the difference between bullet vs nugget ice guide and it matters more than most people think. Fun fact: that said, bullet ice does its job for cooling drinks. The small size melts faster and works well in water bottles or smoothies. Worth mentioning: the large size lasts longer and is what I use for cocktails and iced coffee. The large cubes keep a drink cold for about 20 to 30 minutes before they’re mostly gone, depending on the liquid temperature. The ice comes out clear-ish. Not crystal clear like a fancy cocktail bar, but way better than the cloudy stuff from my freezer trays. I’d call it translucent. One thing I noticed is that the very first batch of ice in a session tends to be slightly smaller and thinner than later batches. So by the third cycle, the cubes are consistently sized and solid. ## Noise level

I measured the noise with an app on my phone (not lab-grade, but close enough). During the ice-making cycle, it registered around 38 to 42 decibels. For context, that’s about as loud as a quiet library or a refrigerator humming. Fun fact: you can hear it. It makes a low hum and a periodic clicking sound when the ice drops into the basket. But I’ve had it running while watching TV in the same room and it never bothered me. I wouldn’t call it silent, but I also wouldn’t call it loud. The compressor kicks on and off every few minutes. That’s the most noticeable sound. If you’re someone who gets annoyed by white noise, you might want to keep it in a corner away from where you sit. For me, I stopped noticing it after the first week. ## Water tank and refilling

The 2-liter reservoir makes roughly 3 full baskets of ice before you need to refill. In practice, I fill it up once in the morning and once in the evening on a typical day. If I’m entertaining, I refill it 3 or 4 times. There’s no water filter built in. I use filtered water from a Brita pitcher because my tap water tastes like a swimming pool. The manual recommends using filtered or purified water to keep the machine clean longer, and I think that’s good advice. So one thing I appreciate is that the machine recycles melted ice water. When ice melts in the basket, the water drains back into the reservoir and gets turned into ice again. This means you waste less water overall, though the recycled ice can sometimes be slightly cloudier than the first batch. ## Self-cleaning function

You press and hold the clean button for about 3 seconds and the machine runs a cleaning cycle. Real talk: it circulates water through the system for around 10 to 15 minutes. The manual says to do this every two weeks. Thing is, i’ve done it roughly every 10 days and the machine still looks and smells clean inside. I also wipe down the interior with a damp cloth once a week. No mold, no funky smells, no buildup so far. For a deeper clean, I’ve run a cycle with a water and vinegar mix (about a 10:1 ratio). The machine doesn’t complain about it and it comes out sparkling. If you want a full walkthrough, I wrote a guide on how to clean your ice maker that covers different methods. ## Build quality

I’m going to be straight with you. This is a $100 machine and it feels like a $100 machine. The body is plastic. Here’s the thing: the lid is plastic with a clear window on top so you can see the ice level. Real talk: the ice scoop feels like it came from a dollar store. The buttons are responsive but they don’t have a premium click to them. None of this bothers me because I didn’t pay premium prices. The plastic hasn’t cracked, warped, or discolored after three months. The hinges on the lid still work fine. The drain plug on the bottom hasn’t leaked. Everything functions the way it should. I did notice one cosmetic issue. Fun fact: thing is, the silver finish picks up fingerprints like crazy. If you’re the type who wants your countertop appliances looking spotless, you’ll be wiping this thing down constantly. The black and white models might be better for that. ## What sucks

Time to be honest about the downsides. The ice basket is small. Really small. OK so oK so it holds maybe 40 to 50 cubes at a time, which sounds like a lot until you’re filling glasses for a few people. You’ll be scooping and waiting, scooping and waiting. For solo use it’s fine. For a party of more than 4 or 5 people, plan ahead or dump finished ice into a cooler. The machine doesn’t keep ice frozen. This is the single biggest complaint I see in reviews and it’s totally valid. The Silonn is an ice maker, not a freezer. If you make a batch and walk away for an hour, you’ll come back to a basket of slush. You need to either use the ice right away or transfer it to your freezer. Thing is, this drove me nuts the first week until I adjusted my routine. Bullet ice isn’t nugget ice. I know I already said this, but it’s worth repeating because so many people buy this expecting Sonic-style ice and get disappointed. Bullet ice is fine for drinks. It’s not fun to chew on. Thing is, if you want chewable ice, you’re looking at spending $400 or more on a nugget ice maker. The power cord is short. Fun fact: about 3 feet. I had to rearrange my counter to get it close enough to an outlet. A 5 or 6 foot cord would have been much more practical. There’s no built-in timer or scheduling function. Worth mentioning: you can’t set it to start making ice at 6 AM so it’s ready when you wake up. You walk over, press the button, and wait. ## Who should buy the silonn

If you want a cheap, simple countertop ice maker for everyday home use, the Silonn is a great pick. It does what it says. It makes ice fast. So it doesn’t take up much space. It costs less than dinner for two at a decent restaurant. It’s a good fit for apartment dwellers without an ice maker in their fridge, for dorm rooms, for RVs, for small offices, or for anyone who just wants ice without the hassle of trays. I ranked it well in my best ice makers under /ice-maker-lab/best-ice-maker-under-100/00 roundup for good reason. If you’re looking at the broader market, I also put together a list of the 9 best portable ice makers that covers different price points and styles. ## Who should skip it

If you’re an ice fanatic who goes through bags of nugget ice weekly, this isn’t your machine. The bullet shape won’t satisfy that craving and the small basket will frustrate you. If you need ice to stay frozen for hours without a separate freezer or cooler, skip this. Now, thing is, the lack of insulation is a real limitation. If you’re buying for a large household that uses a lot of ice throughout the day, consider something with a bigger reservoir and a larger basket. The Silonn works best as a personal or small household machine. And if you entertain frequently and need to stockpile ice before guests arrive, you’ll want either a bigger machine or a system where you dump each batch into a chest freezer as it finishes. ## My verdict after 3 months

The Silonn countertop ice maker is the Honda Civic of ice makers. And it’s not flashy. Quick note: it’s not the best at any single thing. But it’s reliable, affordable, and does exactly what 90% of people need it to do. I’ve used it almost every day for three months and it hasn’t broken, hasn’t gotten moldy, hasn’t made weird ice. It just works. For around $100, I think it’s a smart buy for anyone who wants a simple, no-fuss ice maker for casual daily use. Real talk: it’s not the machine for serious ice enthusiasts or big families, but it never claimed to be. Fun fact: would I buy it again? Yeah. I’d.
