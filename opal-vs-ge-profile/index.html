Opal ice maker vs GE Profile: which one?

2025-05-20 · Reviews

Opal ice maker vs GE profile: which one should you actually buy? if you’ve been shopping for a nugget ice maker, you’ve probably run into some confusing product listings. there’s the “opal ice maker” and then there’s the “GE profile opal 2.0.” are they the same thing? different brands? Fun fact: oK so a knockoff? i get it. the naming is a mess, and it trips up almost everyone. i’ve tested both machines side by side in my kitchen for over three months, and i’m going to break down exactly what separates them, what stayed the same, and which one deserves your counter space. ## the naming confusion, explained

Here’s the short version. The original Opal Nugget Ice Maker was created by FirstBuild, which is actually a subsidiary of GE Appliances. It launched around 2015 through an Indiegogo campaign and became a cult favorite almost overnight. People went nuts for it because it made soft, chewable nugget ice at home, something that previously required a commercial machine or a trip to Sonic. Fast forward a few years, and GE folded the Opal into their GE Profile line. The result is the GE Profile Opal 2.0, a redesigned, upgraded version of the original. Now, same brand family, different generation. Think of it like iPhone 14 vs iPhone 15. Here’s the thing: same company, newer model, some real improvements, higher price tag. You can still find the original Opal floating around on Amazon and occasionally at retail clearance. The GE Profile Opal 2.0 is the current production model. For a deeper look at the newer model specifically, check out my GE Opal review. ## Ice quality

Let’s start with what matters most. Both machines produce genuine nugget ice. It’s the soft, chewable, slightly porous stuff you find at Chick-fil-A or Sonic Drive-In. Thing is, if you’ve ever crunched on a Sonic ice pellet and thought “I need this at home,” either machine will get you there. That said, the 2.0 produces slightly more uniform nuggets. The original Opal sometimes spit out pieces that were a bit too large or oddly shaped. So not a dealbreaker, but if you’re picky about consistency, the 2.0 wins here. The texture is essentially identical on both. Soft, satisfying, perfect for cocktails and sodas. If you’re specifically looking for an ice maker to up your drink game, I’ve got a separate guide on the best ice makers for cocktails. ## Production speed

The original Opal produces about 24 pounds of ice per day. The GE Profile Opal 2.0 bumps that up to around 38 pounds per day. So that’s a significant jump. In my testing, the original took roughly 20 minutes to start producing its first batch of ice. The 2.0 got going in about 15 minutes. Look, over the course of a full day, the 2.0 consistently outproduced the original by a wide margin. If you’re hosting a party or you’ve a large household that burns through ice, the extra output makes a real difference. ## Size and design

The original Opal has a slightly more industrial look. It’s a chunky countertop unit that weighs about 44 pounds. Not ugly, but not exactly sleek either. The GE Profile Opal 2.0 got a design overhaul. It’s a bit more polished, with cleaner lines and a more modern aesthetic. It weighs roughly the same, and the footprint is similar. Worth mentioning: neither machine is small. You’re giving up a solid chunk of counter real estate regardless of which one you pick. Make sure you measure your space before buying. One design change worth calling out: the 2.0 has a larger built-in bin that holds about 3 pounds of ice. The original holds a bit less. Honestly, it doesn’t sound like much, but it means fewer trips to empty or use the ice before it melts back into water and gets recycled. ## The side tank

Both models can use an optional side tank that attaches to the machine and roughly triples the water reservoir capacity. Look, with the original Opal, this was a separate purchase. With some versions of the 2.0, it comes included in the box, though this varies by retailer and bundle. The side tank is honestly one of the best accessories for either machine. Without it, you’re refilling the reservoir constantly, especially if you’re running the machine all day. I’d consider it a must-have for both models. ## Noise

Neither machine is whisper-quiet. They both use a compressor and an auger mechanism to produce ice, which means you’ll hear a consistent hum and occasional grinding sound. The original Opal is noticeably louder. I measured it at around 55 decibels during peak operation, which is roughly the volume of a normal conversation. Honestly, the 2.0 brought that down to about 45 to 50 decibels. Still audible, but much less intrusive. If your kitchen is open to your living room and you watch TV at night, you’ll appreciate the quieter motor on the 2.0. ## WiFi and bluetooth connectivity

The original Opal had Bluetooth connectivity that let you control the machine from your phone. Quick note: it worked, but the app was clunky and the Bluetooth range was limited. You basically had to be in the same room. The GE Profile Opal 2.0 upgraded to WiFi connectivity through the SmartHQ app. And this means you can start your ice maker from anywhere and set schedules the bin is full or the reservoir is low. It also works with Alexa and Google Assistant. Quick note: is smart connectivity a must-have for an ice maker? Honestly, no. But the scheduling feature is legitimately useful. Now, i set mine to start making ice an hour before I usually get home from work, so there’s a full bin waiting when I walk in the door. That’s a nice quality-of-life upgrade over the original’s basic Bluetooth. ## Comparison table

Price

This is where things get interesting. The GE Profile Opal 2.0 retails for around $550, though you can often find it between $480 and $580 depending on sales and whether the side tank is included. The original Opal, when it was actively sold, went for around $450 to $500. Now that it’s being phased out, you can sometimes find it for $350 or even less as retailers clear inventory. Now, refurbished units pop up for under $300. That price gap matters. If you find an original Opal for $350 or less, it’s still a great nugget ice maker. But you’re giving up some production speed, getting a louder machine, and losing WiFi. But the core experience, soft chewable nugget ice on your counter, is still there. ## Reliability and warranty

I’ll be straightforward: both models have had their share of reliability complaints. The original Opal was notorious for leaking issues and occasional compressor failures after 12 to 18 months. GE addressed some of these problems in later production runs, but it remained a sore spot. The 2.0 improved build quality, but it’s not bulletproof either. I’ve seen reports of similar issues, though at a lower rate. Both models come with a standard one-year limited warranty, which feels thin for a machine in this price range. Real talk: i’d honestly recommend buying from a retailer with a good return policy, or picking up an extended warranty if one is available at a reasonable price. One thing that helps with longevity on either model: clean it regularly. Real talk: run a cleaning cycle with the recommended solution every two weeks if you use it daily. Mineral buildup is the number one killer of these machines, and it’s completely preventable. ## Who should buy the original opal? And if you spot the original Opal on clearance or refurbished for under $350, and you don’t care about WiFi or faster ice production, grab it. It still makes excellent nugget ice. For a single person or a couple who just wants nugget ice for evening drinks, it produces plenty. Fun fact: just know that replacement parts might get harder to find as the model ages out. ## Who should buy the GE profile opal 2.0? Worth mentioning: most people. If you’re buying new today at full price, the 2.0 is the obvious pick. It’s quieter, faster, smarter, and better built. The WiFi scheduling alone justifies the price bump for me. Real talk: if you’re looking at the bigger picture of best nugget ice makers options on the market, the Opal 2.0 consistently ranks at or near the top. ## My verdict

The GE Profile Opal 2.0 wins this comparison. It’s the better machine in almost every measurable category. The 60% jump in daily ice production, the quieter operation, and the WiFi connectivity make it a meaningful generational upgrade, not just a cosmetic refresh. But I want to be clear: the original Opal isn’t bad. Real talk: it started the entire countertop nugget ice craze for a reason. If budget is your primary concern and you find one at a steep discount, you’ll still be happy with it. You’re getting 90% of the experience for potentially 60% of the price. Worth mentioning: for anyone starting fresh and willing to pay full retail, get the 2.0. You’ll use this machine almost every day, and the upgrades make daily life with it noticeably better. There’s something good out there for every budget.
